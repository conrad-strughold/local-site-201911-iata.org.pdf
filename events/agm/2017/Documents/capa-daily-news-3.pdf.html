T

here is a deep irony in the coincidence of Qatar’s near-

isolation simultaneously with the IATA Director General 

and CEO’s statement in his Report on the Global Air 

Transport Industry for 2017 on 05-Jun-2017: “Aviation 

is globalisation at its very best. But to deliver aviation’s many 

benefits we need borders that are open to people and trade”.

He was speaking in the context of the populist upheaval in the 

US and Europe and the threat to globalisation of many recent 

political events.

But the importance of that concept of open borders has been 

magnified by the abrupt action by Saudi Arabia and some allies 

to prevent overflights of their territory; this was despite Saudi’s 

treaty commitments under the International Air Transit Agreement 

(which commits contracting parties to allow overflights by foreign 

airlines).

It has taken 70 years of dedication to globalisation to produce 

the remarkably robust global airline industry that we have today. 

It has its faults, but given its complexity and its vastly varying 

aspects, it is remarkable in its holistic delivery. It has been 

challenged over the years, by safety concerns, by security threats, 

pandemics, volcanoes, economic uncertainty; commercially, by 

being constrained by artificial and unwieldy regulation.

At the same time, pulling down that system can be the matter 

of a few short moments. Protecting that fragile industry, one that 

is so vital to world trade, is brought into clear focus when air 

services can be cut so peremptorily.

As Mr de Juniac observed, “today, 11 million people will trust 

our companies to transport them safely. And our aircraft will 

be full with 150,000 tonnes of cargo—the input and output of 

the global economy. This is our industry at work—an engine of 

commerce”.

Moreover, every part of that vital system is interconnected. 

What happens in Qatar aviation will affect travellers in dozens 

of countries; freight carriage will be disrupted. There can be few 

winners, if any, in such cases but many losers.

The larger issue of globalisation is an essential goal for an 

industry that relies so evidently on the freedom to move. That 

applies to each part of the chain. Where IATA has played such a 

valuable role over the decades has been in providing a forum for 

discussion – and action – in the common interest. It is a role that 

is becoming harder by the day.

But it is indeed the business of freedom. That is a business 

worth pursuing at all cost.

Addressing “the business of freedom”

PETER HARBISON

EXECUTIVE CHAIRMAN

About

The International Air Transport Association (IATA) is an international trade association for the world’s airlines, established in 

1945 with 57 founding members. Today, IATA represents more than 275 airlines in 117 countries, comprising 83% of scheduled 

international air traffic. Its mission is, “to represent, lead and serve the airline industry.”
IATA has 54 offices in 53 countries, with its Head Office in Montreal, Executive Office in Geneva and Regional Offices in Am-

man, Beijing, Brussels, Johannesburg, Madrid, Miami, Moscow, Singapore and Washington. The IATA Annual General Meeting 

& World Air Transport Summit, held in June each year, formalises industry positions on industry and public policy issues and 

provides a focus for emerging industry issues.

IATA: Latin America traffic to double by 2034, could be 

better if roadblocks are removed

IATA regional VP the Americas Peter Cerda, speaking at the 2017 

IATA AGM, released (04-Jun-2017) the following overview for the 

Latin America & the Caribbean regional aviation industry:

• Generates USD167 billion in GDP, with positive regional economic 

outlook. Contribution to regional GDP could jump to USD322 

billion, but only if industry can grow unhindered and roadblocks 

are addressed;

• Passenger growth expected to double by 2034, fuelled by lower 

airfares, rising incomes and demographic growth;

• Supports 5.2 million jobs;

• Connects the region with 160 global cities;

• 2.6 million flights p/a;

• Country specific challenges: 

• Brazil: Carriers are recovering but political instability continues;

• Cuba: Demand weaker than expected. Opportunities remain;

• Mexico: Challenges in passenger rights and slots;

• Venezuela: National crisis continues to worsen;

• Chile, Panama, Peru and Colombia: Forecast well in 2017.

discussions key at 2017 IATA AGM

 

Aeromexico CEO Andres Conesa, speaking ahead of the 2017

emerging”, as well as new issues such as restrictions to travel. It is

IATA: Laptop ban not the right measure to deal with 

security threat

 

IATA DG and CEO Alexandre de Juniac, speaking ahead of the 

2017 IATA AGM, said that the association opposes restrictions on 

the transport of large electronic devices in the cabins of aircraft 

(Reuters, 02-Jun-2017). Mr de Juniac said IATA has “no doubt” about 

the security threat posed, but the association has doubts about 

the security measures introduced in response. The US and the UK 

introduced the ban on large electronic devices in Mar-2017, covering 

selected airports in the Middle East, North Africa and Turkey.

IATA: US and Europe protectionist rhetoric could slow 

air travel demand

IATA DG and CEO Alexandre de Juniac, speaking ahead of the 2017 

IATA AGM, said talk of protectionism coming from Europe and the US 

could slow demand for air travel, and the association considers any 

“barrier to borders” as a “threat” to travel (Reuters, 02-Jun-2017).

IATA: Airline industry ‘strongly mobilised to fight 

against’ human trafficking 

 

IATA DG and CEO Alexandre de Juniac, speaking ahead of the 2017 

IATA AGM, said the airline industry is treating human trafficking and 

migrant smuggling with “growing concern” and it is an issue that the 

industry is “strongly mobilised to fight against” (Reuters, 04-Jun-2017). 

Airline industry leaders will formally discuss the issue at the 04/06-

Jun-2017 IATA AGM.

IATA: Latin America needs to better infrastructure and 

regulation to maximise aviation benefits

 

IATA urged (04-Jun-2017) Latin America governments to maximise 

the benefits aviation generates by addressing critical infrastructure 

deficiencies and applying ‘Smarter Regulation’ principles. IATA 

Regional VP for the Americas Peter Cerda stated governments in the 

region are “hindering sustainable growth with chronic infrastructure 

deficiencies and debilitating regulation”. Critical examples include 

insufficient capacity at Lima International Airport, antiquated 

infrastructure at Mexico City International Airport and archaic air traffic 

management in Buenos Aires and the surrounding area. Mr Cerda 

also called on governments in the region to ensure regulations are 

intended to “achieve clearly defined, measurable policy objectives 

that can be adhered to in the least burdensome manner possible”, 

while noting that “any regulatory framework benefits from a 

transparent and objective consultative process between governments 

and industry”. Regulations in Brazil and proposed legislation in Mexico 

are examples where governments are undermining growth and 

competitiveness in the sector, according to Mr Cerda.

US domestic load factor reaches April record

 

IATA stated (01-Jun-2017) US domestic traffic increased 4.7% in Apr-

2017, boosted by an “uptick in demand over the past few months, 

alongside indications of strong consumer confidence”. The US load 

factor posted an all time high for the month of April of 85.6%. 

IATA: Russia posts highest global domestic growth 

rate in Apr-2017

 

IATA stated (01-Jun-2017) Russia’s airlines posted the fastest domestic 

growth rate at 16.7% year-on-year in Apr-2017. IATA stated the growth 

occurred against an improving economic outlook as oil prices have 

firmed, and business confidence indicators “are quite high by recent 

historical standards”. However, capacity growth mostly kept pace with 

demand and load factor was nearly flat at 78.7%. 

IATA: Global demand for domestic travel increases 

7.7% in Apr-2017

 

IATA stated (01-Jun-2017) global demand for domestic travel 

increased 7.7% year-on-year in Apr-2017, while capacity increased 

6.2%. Load factors rose 1.2% to 83%. All markets reported demand 

increases with the exception of Australia, which showed a 2.1% 

decline. 

IATA: African airlines leads growth in Apr-2017

 

IATA stated (01-Jun-2017) African airlines led all regions in growth 

in Apr-2017, with a 17.2% traffic increase. The growth represents 

the fastest pace for African airlines in more than five years. IATA 

noted this follows a recovery in demand on key market routes to 

Europe. IATA however stated conditions in the continent’s two 

largest economies are diverging, with business confidence rising 

in Nigeria, while political uncertainty remains heightened in South 

Africa. Capacity rose 6.1% and load factors increase 6.9% to 72.5% in 

Apr-2017.

IATA: North American airline demand growth reflects 

‘robust’ economic backdrop, strong USD

 

IATA stated (01-Jun-2017) North American airlines posted a 10.3% 

demand increase year-on-year in Apr-2017. IATA stated the rise 

“appears to reflect a combination of the comparatively robust 

economic backdrop and the strength of the US dollar”. Capacity rose 

5.8%, and load factor increased 3.3% points to 81.8%.

IATA: Electronic device ban ‘appears to have weighed 

down’ Middle East-North America traffic

 

IATA stated (01-Jun-2017) the cabin ban on the carriage of large 

portable electronic devices from 10 Middle Eastern and African 

airports to the US “appears to have weighed down Middle East-North 

America passenger traffic” in Apr-2017. IATA DG and CEO Alexandre 

de Juniac said: “There are indications that passengers are avoiding 

routes where the large PED ban is in place. As the US Department 

of Homeland Security considers expanding the ban, the need to find 

alternative measures to keep flying secure is critical. If the ban were 

extended to Europe-to-US flights, for example, we estimate a USD1.4 

billion hit on productivity”.

IATA urges airline participation in Flight Data 

eXchange programme

 

IATA urged (01-Jun-2017) airlines to participate in its Flight Data 

eXchange (FDX) programme. FDX is a safety initiative created 

to identify systemic issues and trends, by using a collection of 

aggregated de-identified flight data provided by participating airlines. 

The programme also enables airlines to benchmark their safety 

performance against the industry, identify existing safety risks, and 

risks at potential new destinations. As an industry tool, the current 

focus is on analysing five areas: misconfigured take-off, mid-air 

collision risk, loss of control risk, controlled flight into terrain risk, and 

approach and landing risk. IATA flight data analysis manager Bruno 

Ochin added: “The more carriers participate, the more valuable FDX 

becomes”.

IATA: Airfreight growth remains above five year 

average in Apr-2017, 7.5% growth expected for 2017

 

IATA reported (01-Jun-2017) global FTKs grew 8.5% year-on-year in 

Apr-2017, down from 13.4% growth in Mar-2017 but remaining above 

the average annual rate of 3.5% over the last five years. All regions 

except Latin America recorded growth in demand. Airlines in Asia 

Pacific and Europe accounted for more than 70% of growth. IATA 

said business confidence indicators remain upbeat, suggesting FTK 

growth will remain robust for the rest of 2Q2017, but there are signs 

the cyclical growth peak for air cargo has passed, particularly given 

the inventory-to-sales ratio stopped falling at the end of 2016. For the 

full year 2017, IATA said “air freight is headed for a healthy growth 

rate of 7.5%, supported by strong pharmaceuticals and e-commerce”. 

IATA director general and CEO Alexandre de Juniac commented: 

“Growth rates... are still much more robust than anything we have 

seen in the last six years. That’s good news, but it should not be taken 

as a message that all is well in air cargo. The industry’s antiquated 

processes need modernisation.” IATA noted the following regional 

details:

• Africa: Recorded the largest regional demand growth at 26%. 

Demand was boosted by very strong growth on trade lanes to and 

from Asia, which increased nearly 55% for the year to date. Load 

factor remains challenged and was the lowest of all regions;

• Asia Pacific: Volume growth of 8.4% reflects the strength of 

exporters’ order books across the region. Seasonally adjusted 

volumes recently surpassed levels reached in the 2010 bounce-back 

following the global financial crisis;

• Europe: Volume growth slowed slightly compared to Mar-2017, but 

remained well above the five year average. International volumes 

increased 13.1%. The weakness of the Euro continues to boost 

performance of the freight market, which benefitted from strong 

export orders in recent months;

• Latin America: Recovery in seasonally adjusted volumes stalled, with 

Apr-2017 demand approaching a seven year low. Demand is now 14% 

lower than the peak in 2014. Carriers have adjusted capacity, limiting 

the negative impact on load factor;

• Middle East: Growth slowed sharply from 16.3% in Mar-2017 to 

3.1% in Apr-2017. The drop appears to be related to volatile monthly 

developments in 2016. International volumes increased 8% for the 

year to Apr-2017, slower than the five year average of 11%. Seasonally 

adjusted volumes maintained an upward trend. Middle East-Europe 

demand remains strong, but traffic to Asia weakened;

• North America: Seasonally adjusted volumes jumped in Apr-2017, 

but it is too soon to tell if this will continue. The strength of the US 

dollar continues to boost the inbound freight market but is keeping 

the export market under pressure.

the demand growth in Apr-2017.

IATA Economic Outlook: Air freight rebound driven by 

re-stocking 

 

IATA chief economist Brian Pearce, speaking at the IATA AGM, noted 

(05-Jun-2017) that since 2010 “life has been pretty gloomy for the 

air cargo industry” but that light has started to appear at the end of 

the tunnel for the industry. “A rebound in air cargo growth seems to 

be solidly supported. In this case by a need for retailers and industry 

to restock their inventories in the face of the upturn in business 

confidence and economic growth. Whenever there is a fall in the 

inventory sales ratio – driven by unexpectedly strong sales – then 

business turns to rapid air transport to restock components and 

finished goods”, he said. 

IATA Economic Outlook: Global load factors are 

mostly at all-time highs 

 

IATA chief economist Brian Pearce, speaking at the IATA AGM, noted 

(05-Jun-2017) that global load factors are at all-time highs across 

most regions of the world, with the exception of the Middle East. 

“The importance of a surge in traffic growth for airline performance 

is that it is producing record load factors for airlines in most regions”, 

he explained. “Even African airlines are seeing the benefit currently.” 

The Middle East exception when comparing Apr-2017 to Apr-2016 

performance reflects “a number of factors specific to the region”, 

he noted, partly putting this down to the general evolution of the air 

transport industry and challenges linked to market growth, including 

aircraft orders and capacity growing ahead of the demand curve. “It 

is like in China. You are not going to see these high rates of growth 

forever”, he added

IATA DG: Airlines face headwinds from nationalistic 

rhetoric and cracks in security relationships 

 

IATA DG and CEO Alexandre de Juniac, speaking at the 2017 IATA 

AGM, issued (05-Jun-2017) his report on the state of the global air 

transport industry. Key highlights include:

• Airlines will accommodate more then 4 billion passengers in 2017, 

contributing USD2.7 in economic activity, handling a third of global 

trade by value and supporting 63 million jobs. Over half of tourists 

arrive by air;

• Aviation as a business depends on borders that are open to people 

and trade. The industry faces headwinds “from those who would deny 

the benefits of globalization”. Mr de Juniac warned that in parts of the 

world “nationalistic political rhetoric points towards a future of more 

protectionism”;

• Safety: Remains the industry’s top priority. In 2016 there were 40.4 

million flight sectors and 10 fatal accidents. Gains have been made in 

safety, with the number of airlines committing to the IATA Operational 

Safety Audit extending “far beyond our membership” and reporting 

safety performance nearly 50% better than the record of those not 

on the registry. Mr de Juniac warned that “we have taken a step back 

in air accident investigations” and urged governments to do better. 

Of the 1,000 accidents investigated over the last decade, reports are 

available for only 300;

• Sustainability: The 2016  Carbon Offset and Reduction Scheme for 

International Aviation (CORSIA) agreement was a”giant step forward 

in meeting our sustainability commitments, particularly carbon-neutral 

growth from 2020”. 70 states have already joined, although the 

scheme will be voluntary until 2026. The decision of the US to back 

out of the Paris Agreement “is not a setback for CORSIA” as the two 

are completely separate agreements. Mr de Juniac warned that the 

alternative to CORSIA is a “patchwork of measures that would be 

ineffective, costly and unmanageable”. Top sustainability priorities are: 

o The commercialization of sustainable alternative aviation fuels;

o Quickly nailing down the details of CORSIA with enough time for 

implementation by airlines;

o Modernizing air traffic management.

• Financial performance: IATA forecasts airlines will generate USD31.4 

billion in profit on USD743 billion in revenues in 2017, a “sound 

performance”. Airlines a generating above-cost-of-capital returns 

for investors of 8.8%. Air cargo has “awakened from a six-year 

coma” with 7.5% growth forecast “powered by e-commerce and 

pharmaceutical shipments”. Profitability remains unevenly spread, 

with half the industry’s profits are being made in North America;

• Security: Aviation remain focused on safety and security, but the 

necessary relationships with governments are “showing cracks”. The 

US and UK large electronic device ban was introduced “by surprise” 

with “no consultation with industry and little time to implement.” 

IATA estimates it has cost airline passengers USD180 million in lost 

productivity. The ban “must change” with measures that are “logical, 

effective and efficient”; 

o Mr de Junica said his message to governments on security is 

one of partnership, asking them to work with the airline industry. 

He noted that this could be addressed through ICAO’s efforts 

to establish a Global Aviation Security Plan (GASeP) to lay the 

groundwork for more effective information-sharing among states 

and with the industry—the key to addressing current and future 

threats.

IATA makes minor upgrade to 2017 profit outlook as 

labour, fuel and supplier costs rise

 

IATA released (05-Jun-2017) its 2017 industry profitability outlook at 

the 2017 IATA AGM, raising the profit outlook upwards from USD29.8 

billion to USD31.4 billion, and the outlook for revenues from USD736 

to USD743 billion. Net profit per passenger is forecast at USD7.69, 

down 15.8% year-on-year and 21% from 2015. Average net profit 

margin stands at 4.2% (down from 4.9% in 2016). Key highlights 

include:

• Geographic imbalance: About 50% of industry profits are being 

generated in North America (USD15.4 billion). Carriers in Europe and 

the Asia Pacific will each add USD7.4 billion in profit. Latin America 

and Middle East carriers are expected to earn USD800 million and 

USD400 million respectively. Airlines in Africa are expected to post a 

USD100 million loss;

• Stronger than expected demand environment: Expectations for GDP 

growth in 2017 stand at 2.9%. If realised this will be the strongest 

global economic performance since 2011;

• Passenger demand: Expected to grow by 7.4%, matching 2016. The 

stronger demand translates into an additional 275 million passengers 

(over 2016), bringing total traffic to 4.1 billion. If achieved, this would 

be the largest year-on-year growth in absolute passenger numbers 

ever recorded. Average passenger load factor is expected to reach 

80.6% up 0.3 ppts, helping to boost unit revenues;

• Cargo demand: Expected to grow by 7.5% in 2017, more than double 

the 3.6% growth realised in 2016 and 4.0 ppts above the previous 

forecast for 2-17. Total cargo carried is expected to reach 58.2 

million tonnes, growth of 6.3% and 2.5 million tonnes higher than the 

previous forecast;

• Costs accelerating: Cost increases for fuel, labour and maintenance 

accelerated in 1Q2017. Overall industry expenses are expected to 

rise to USD687 billion, +6% year-on-year. The total industry fuel bill 

is predicted to be USD129 billion, down 3%. The forecast anticipates 

an average oil price of USD54 per barrel for Brent crude., +21%, 

reflecting a “broad balance between OPEC supply cuts and new 

supply from US shale oil producers”. Fuel will account for 18.8% of the 

industry’s total costs;

• Yields forecast to decline: Yields are still expected decline 2% 

over 2017, but there are signs of stabilisation during 1H2017, driven 

by better capacity utilisation and the imperative to respond to the 

rise of unit costs. The 2% decline is the smallest decrease in recent 

years, after a 8.8% fall in 2016 and an 11.9% fall in 2015. Cargo yields 

are expected to fall by 1.0% over 2017, also the smallest decrease in 

recent years.

SAS was ‘fighting for its life’ acknowledges CEO
