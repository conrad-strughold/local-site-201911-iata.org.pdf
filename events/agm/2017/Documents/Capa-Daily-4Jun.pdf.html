I

N THE CONTINUING WAR between protectionism and 

globalisation, many battles have been fought.

It is a highly nuanced war and there are many ways of 

winning. And losing. 

For the airline industry it is not always clear who are the 

real winners and losers. It is a cutthroat business in which 

there are necessarily those who perform better than others, 

regardless of the state of liberalisation and global market 

access.

It is not an even battle within that industry; in fact the 

airline business is characterised by artificial distortions. In 

this most global of industries, there are uniquely archaic 

and unhelpful hurdles that divert the natural forces of the 

marketplace.

In many ways the past two decades of liberalisation 

has helped reduce the impact of these anachronisms, as 

consumer interests have come to occupy a greater place in 

definitions of the “national interest”.

But globalisation provides a bigger background, the goal 

of integrating and connecting national markets. Measures 

like aviation open skies agreements are enhanced as 

these philosophies spread, but they also have a separate 

existence.

We asked IATA DG and CEO, Alexandre de Juniac, a 

few questions against a background of alarming signs 

of a reversion to the bad old days of trade barriers and 

nationalism. 

Although logic – and extensive bodies of studies – 

support the value of open trade and the extensive economic 

and social benefits that globalisation delivers, there are 

always some losers. Appealing to nationalism and to the 

generally disaffected is a popular, sometimes successful, 

political tactic especially in an age of social media and fake 

news. 

It is against this background that IATA’s World Air 

Transport Summit will look at the potential impact of the 

erosion of globalisation.

As the world’s leading airline trade body, IATA has a 

powerful mandate to lobby for the interests of its members 

– which increasingly tend to coincide with the wider 

interests of travellers and shippers, at least insofar as open 

trades are concerned.

Mr de Juniac’s message is that persistent appeals backed 

by fact based arguments are the main tool of the industry 

in securing its future. Persuading the willingly deaf is not 

always easy and even the purest of logic cannot necessarily 

convince political leaders whose short term interests do not 

coincide. But the message is so compelling and extensively 

valid that pursuing the theme must surely be effective in the 

long run – providing all airlines sing from the same hymn 

sheet. 

It can hardly be beneficial to that cause for divisive public 

attacks on competitor airlines to be made, appealing at 

the same time to the same nationalistic tendencies that 

threaten the future of globalisation.

rules will decline in importance, also noted that IATA has

CAPA:

 In its 2017 outlook IATA cited protectionism as one of the main headwinds facing the 

aviation industry, probably contributing to slower industry growth this year. And earlier this 

year you said: “we must all be concerned about the protectionist rhetoric that is spreading. 

Aviation is the business of freedom. The industry is premised on borders that are open to 

people and trade; that is at the heart of the important role that we play in globalisation.” 

Is there much that IATA (and its members) can do to head off these pressures? If so, what are 

the most powerful weapons IATA has?

Mr de Juniac:

 Our greatest power is that of persuasion. Aviation supports about USD3.3 

trillion in economic activity. And there are at least 70 million jobs in the world that would not 

exist were it not for aviation. Moreover, aviation delivers, by value, about a third of goods 

traded internationally. Those are powerful facts and we are doing our best to get them onto 

the radar screen of decision-makers—directly, through the reach of our membership and 

via our partners in industry. Political leaders need to understand what is at stake—jobs and 

prosperity.

One of our key tools of persuasion are well-researched studies.  Two recent examples 

supporting open borders are our study of the impact of Brexit, the negative impact of 

increased trade barriers once the UK leaves the EU single market, and the value of air cargo 

which examined the value to economic development of Global Value Chains.

It is a valid viewpoint that the tide of globalization has not lifted all ships equally. But it is 

undeniable that globalization has made the world a more prosperous place. And we have a 

duty to remind governments of that.

original purpose. Many airlines are engaging in equity purchases and various forms of cross 

border joint venture that potentially undermine the fundamentals of O&C.

(i) Do you see the O&C provisions largely disappearing, say, by 2025?

(i) Does IATA have a formal position on whether or not the provisions should be preserved/

removed?

Mr de Juniac: 

Such rules can only be changed by governments. And I don’t see a great 

willingness among governments to address ownership and control restrictions in a meaningful 

way. We see examples of investment caps rising to 49%. And there are a few very rare cases 

where a foreign investor can own a local domestic airline pretty much outright. But that breaks 

down when confronted with the bilateral system governing international operations. We have 

published a number of studies in the past showing the wider economic benefits for countries 

from removing these provisions. 

 I do believe that the situation will change with time.  But I would not put a bet on 2025.

CAPA: 

One of the ways airlines have sought to circumvent the national restrictions is through 

global alliances. These have been hugely successful for some of their members, but there 

is much discussion about where they will head, as airlines seek ever more specific bilateral 

partnerships. 

There is also the issue of:

How do you see the future evolution of the branded global alliances in this environment? 

For example do you anticipate more cross-alliance bilateral partnerships - and how can they be 

reconciled, as where BA and Qantas are each oneworld members, yet have partnerships with 

Qatar Airways and Emirates respectively?

Mr de Juniac:

 Alliances play an important role in the industry. They help carriers to provide 

their customers with access to markets beyond their own network. They help with customer 

loyalty. There is an important cost element fulfilled through joint infrastructure such as 

lounges. I would not want to speculate on their future developments, but what they do today is 

valuable. And I don’t see that changing dramatically, particularly as the partnerships are hard 

wired through IT and infrastructure investments. And they are pretty well-embedded in the 

expectations of consumers.

We are starting to see some bilateral arrangements that cross alliances. I suspect that we will 

see more of that in the future.

In most low-margin, competitive, and capital-intensive industries consolidation is a natural 

development. Through alliances and other forms of cooperation, and within the strict confines 

of anti-trust rules, airlines are finding some more creative ways to achieve similar benefits.

Immunized joint ventures are a development to watch on long-haul markets. They are a key 

part of the strategy of many airlines. The approval of regulators implies that they have seen the 

consumer benefits of increased choice, convenience and efficiency.

In regional markets, we are seeing franchising take on a greater role. It is helping airlines to 

work together and provide more convenient and denser networks. And what is interesting is 

that we see the same development in both new model airlines and traditional network carriers.

CAPA: 

IATA’s position used to be that the general trade rules of the WTO/GATT were not 

suitable for airline operations. Where there are calls for the use of GATT to prevent competition 

distorting practices like subsidies and trade barriers (and perhaps as the ownership and control 

rules are eroded), do you believe IATA’s airlines consider revising its original position? Should 

they?

Mr de Juniac:

 We seem to be in an era where the WTO is making very limited progress. 

There is an established role for it to play with those that provide services or equipment to the 

industry. And we are very optimistic about the WTO’s Trade Facilitation Agreement what will 

help to boost trade by reducing red tape at borders. 

But I don’t see WTO being able to drive a meaningful agreement on air rights. It is a matter of 

practicality, not one of what should or should not be done. The governments are not asking for 

WTO to take this on. And there is little interest among airlines.

Our greatest power is that of persuasion.

disbandment and obliteration.

global alliances have been busy creating a new life. This report 

looks at the three global alliances’ evolution of strategy and recent 

changes in management. Alliances are being tasked with three 

pillars to facilitate and support: joint-ventures, unbundling and how 

to grow membership, mostly in the LCC/hybrid space. Low-cost 

alliances are briefly considered.

The opportunities for global alliances to bring together and support 

member airlines and their passengers are greater than ever before. 

Yet cohesiveness at some airlines will require exclusion with other 

members. This will create casualties, with resulting changes possibly 

setting off alliance membership musical chairs....

United Airlines to launch Denver-Cozumel service 

 

United Airlines plans to launch weekly Denver-Cozumel 

service on 17-Dec-2017, as per a 23-May-2017 GDS inventory 

and timetable display. The carrier will operate the seasonal 

route through 07-Apr-2017, utilising Boeing 737-800 aircraft. 

United will be the sole operator on the route, according to 

OAG. Frontier Airlines previously operated the route on a 

weekly basis in 2015.
