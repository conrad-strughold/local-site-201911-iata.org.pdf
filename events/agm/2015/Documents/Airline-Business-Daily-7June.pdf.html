Association of Asia Paciﬁc Air-

“If you look at last year, capaci-

ty outstripped demand roughly by 
1-2% and load factors fell accord-

there is that slight overcapacity 
meant that airlines had no pricing 
power and yields fell.”

As a result number of full-ser-

vice and low-cost carriers have 
adjusted capacity, largely through 
delivery deferrals and aircraft 
sales. The drop in fuel prices 
should also help to boost margins 
once carriers work through their 
hedging positions. Demand also 
remains strong, at around 5-7% 
per annum.

“The demand side looks 

healthy, and the cost side will 
mostly be given away in more 

competitive fares, but we should 
see margins improve modestly 
this year,” says Herdman.

There are also signs of some 

 

recovery in the air cargo business, 
which has been on a slow recov-
ery from its collapse in 2010 and 
is key to the Asian sector. Cargo 
volumes in the ﬁrst quarter were 
stronger – aided by strikes at US 
ports – but there is still some over-
capacity due to the continuing 

surplus of maindeck freighters.

Herdman says that AAPA has 

picked up “mixed signals” for the 
outlook of the cargo industry for 
the rest of the year, but he believes 
that it is on the right track for a 
sustained recovery.

“It will be some time before we 

get complete supply-demand bal-
ance but it’s on the mend. I’m an 
optimist on medium-term freight-
er demand,” he adds.

50% of the 155 outstanding ﬁrm 
orders for the aircraft with no 
new customers joining the pro-

gramme since Transaero ordered 
four in 2012, the Ascend Fleets 
database shows.

In addition to seeking new cus-

tomers, the European airframer is 
now dealing with placing two 
“white-tail” aircraft that were

“[Instead of] one ﬂight a day

Rainey, citing London Heathrow 
as a market where frequency is 
king on routes to the USA.

Safety initiatives in the wake of several high-profile disasters are just some of the projects to be advanced in the year ahead

“Airlines do not 
employ spies.  
We need better 
sharing of 
information”

CALIN ROVINESCU

IATA chairman and  

Air Canada chief executive

Dario A

yala

View from the chairman

A

ir Canada chief executive 
Calin Rovinescu has some 

 advice for the incoming chair of 
IATA’s board of governors: think of 
the job as an opportunity to leave 
the industry in better shape than 
how you found it.

Rovinescu says that means driv-

ing “progressive” change – work-
ing with a wide range of parties to 
achieve incremental progress on a 
variety of issues. “The best you can 
do for this industry is to continue 
to assist in transforming it,” Rovi-
nescu tells 

Airline Business Daily

. 

“You hope to turn it over to the 
next board in better shape than it 
was for the previous board.”

The next board will be led by 

Aeromexico chief executive An-
dres Conesa, who takes over when 
Rovinescu’s term ends following 
the annual general meeting.

Rovinescu thinks IATA has 

achieved its goal in the last year. It 
has started to address the need for 
aircraft tracking technology and 
improved sharing of geographic 
risks, he notes. And at the same 
time, IATA has continued its work 
to reduce emissions, improved its 
management of settlement funds, 
addressed distribution issues and 
improved its governance.

SAFETY EFFORTS

Rovinescu, whose tenure coincid-
ed with several high-proﬁle avia-
tion accidents, names the group’s 
safety work as among its most no-
table efforts of the year.

During his time at the helm, 

searchers continued seeking 
wreckage from Malaysia Airlines 
ﬂight MH370, Malaysia ﬂight 
MH17 was shot down over 
Ukraine, Indonesia AirAsia ﬂight 
QZ8501 slammed into the Java 
Sea, TransAsia Airways ﬂight 
GE235 crashed after takeoff from 
Taipei and the co-pilot of German-
wings ﬂight 9525 allegedly ﬂew 
the aircraft into the French Alps.

Despite those crashes, commer-

cial aviation’s safety rate has never 
been higher, but Rovinescu says 
much more can be done.

During his tenure, an IATA task 

force recommended that aircraft 
have tracking systems that report 
positions at 15min intervals, 
though he concedes it will not “be 
a perfect system because of the ca-
pability to disarm those systems”.

Rovinescu thinks most of the 

world’s largest operators will out-
ﬁt their aircraft with such tracking 
systems quickly, but says costs 
may delay worldwide adoption.

“Like anything else that in-

creases the level of safety, it will 
take some time to implement in 
some of the countries that have 
some cost constraints,” he says.

Rovinescu says conﬂict zones 

that pose risks to airliners remain 
“a fairly challenging problem”, 
but that IATA must continue 
working with governments to 
 create a “clearing house” to share 
risk information.

Such a database will be tricky 

as it must not breach countries’ se-
curity protocols, he notes.

“Airlines do not employ spies. 

[We] have to have a much better 
sharing of information between 
states,” he says.

The issue of pilot mental health 

– highlighted by the Germanwings 
tragedy – has also spurred discus-
sions. Although carriers have in-
stituted a two-person cockpit rule, 
Rovinescu says the question now 
is whether medical screening can 
be improved to better account for 
mental health problems.

But stricter mental health rules 

raise issues relating to medical pri-
vacy and information sharing, 
Rovinescu adds. “I think there will 
be some very good studies to see if 
there is anything incremental that 
could be done in addition to the 
existing system of physical checks, 
which [already] get into mental 
health issues,” he says. 

“I don’t think you will see 

 anything change overnight kind of 
because it does raise all kinds of 
other issues.”

While safety has been among 

the group’s most high-proﬁle is-
sues, Rovinescu says IATA contin-
ues to impress upon governments 
worldwide the need for a “global 
emissions scheme”, rather than a 
patchwork of country-by-country 
emissions requirements.

The group has been tackling the 

issue for years, but has made pro-
gress: member states agreed in 
2013 to develop a global market-
based emissions measure for 
adoption in 2016.

GREATER ACCESS

Also during the last year, IATA has 
made the organisation and its 
committees more accessible to all 
carriers, including newer mem-
bers from a range of geographies.

The group has also strength-

ened its board reporting processes 
and standardised documentation, 
ensuring information ﬂows from 
the organisation to the board. 

In addition, Rovinescu says 

IATA has made its ﬁnancial settle-
ment function – which acts as a 
payment clearing house between 
airlines and travel agents – more 
robust and improved how it deals 
with issues like fraud. 

IATA also continues to advance 

its New Distribution Capability”, 
an initiative to create a standard 
that will help ensure the global 
distribution systems (GDSs) can 
display all airlines’ wares, includ-
ing ancillary products.

On top of all those issues, IATA 

continues lobbying for reduced 
aviation taxes, an issue Rovinescu 
calls a “personal pet peeve”.

Through it all, IATA must con-

tinue to remain apolitical, Rovi-
nescu adds, avoiding sides on is-
sues, such as the ongoing spat 
between US carriers and their 
Gulf rivals over alleged govern-
ment subsidies. 

“IATA is a totally independent, 

apolitical organisation,” he says. 
“IATA is intended to make the in-
dustry better, and in my view 
should never express any opinion 
on these topics."

I

ATA touches down in Miami 
for its 2015 annual general 

meeting as the airline industry en-
joys one of its purple patches, 
with proﬁtability in absolute 
terms and load factors heading to 
new highs while low fuel prices 
remain relatively depressed.

While the US/Gulf open-skies 

row will be on everyone’s lips in 
Miami, when it comes to the good 
times none are enjoying them 
more so than those carriers of this 
year’s host nation.

“The industry is going well 

here in the USA, for a number of 
reasons. And the restructuring [at 
the majors] has played a large part 
in that. But obviously time will 
tell how things pan out,” says 
IATA director general Tony Tyler.

He tells 

Airline Business Daily

 

that while the ﬁnancial strength at 
the US carriers will encourage 
them to grow and develop their 
businesses, “expensive lessons” 
have been learnt in the past by air-
line managements that threw cau-
tion to the wind in pursuit of mar-
ket share.

“One imagines that the manage-

ments of these businesses will ex-

pand them prudently and in a 
way that continues to generate re-
turns on their invested capital, 
which I know that they are all set-
ting great store by, rather than per-
haps the old days where market 
share was seen as the main objec-
tive,” Tyler says.

With the outlook in the US air-

line market so rosy at the moment, 
some observers see the conditions 
ripe for a new entrant to disrupt the 
status quo and damage the proﬁta-
bility path of the industry. Tyler 
concurs that “the smell of avgas 
and the excitement of seeing your 
name painted on a big aeroplane 
does attract people to this indus-
try”, but he does not believe the ﬁ-
nances of the industry “are so com-
pelling that anyone with capital to 
spare will see this as an easy win”.

REGIONAL DIFFERENCES

Despite the industry’s relative 
buoyancy, Tyler quickly points 
out that fuel pricing isn’t quite as 
low as last year, and “as always 
,the average [strong global airline 
ﬁnancial performance] hides sig-
niﬁcant variation between regions 
and between airlines. You do need 

to look a little bit deeper than just 
the headline ﬁgures to see what’s 
really going on.”

One big issue that Tyler has had 

to address since last year’s AGM 
was what he calls “the outrage” 
last July when a Malaysia Airlines 
Boeing 777 was shot down over 
Ukraine. While IATA has been 
working with ICAO to establish an 
online database of intelligence 
populated by states, there was 
criticism from some members 
over how the association initially 
reacted to the shoot-down.

“I’m not sure what more we 

could have done, to be honest. It is 
very important that IATA doesn’t 
become a sort of engine of ru-
mours,” says Tyler. “By the end of 
July we had got CANSO and ACI 
to come with us to meet with 
ICAO for a high-level summit.”

Tyler says its longer-term push 

to prevent a repeat of the MH17 
tragedy will take time to bear fruit: 
“This call we are making for inter-
national law to get involved, and 
for a convention that controls the 
design, the manufacture and the 
deployment of weapons that 
could bring down a civilian air-

craft is needed. We will keep the 
pressure on ICAO to push this 
through the UN process.”

At this year’s AGM, IATA is 

proudly celebrating its 70th anni-
versary. Tyler says while the asso-
ciation has evolved hugely over 
those seven decades, it is still well 
aligned with the ideals of its 
founding fathers in 1945 “that the 
industry, by working together, 
could do a lot to promote, develop 
and facilitate the growth of inter-
national travel and commerce by 
air. And over the years I think 
IATA and its members can be very 
proud of what we’ve achieved.

“If we’re in for the same degree 

of change over the next 70 years, 
IATA will still be here operating in 
the areas where the airlines and 
the wider connected world bene-
ﬁts from airline co-operation.”

One area where IATA members 

are currently at loggerheads, rath-
er than co-operating, is around the 
ongoing dispute between the US 
majors and the Gulf carriers over 
the latter’s alleged $42 billion in il-
legal subsidies. Tyler and IATA 
have been at pains to avoid being 
dragged into the row, but the DG 
acknowledges that the debate is 
likely to raise its head in Miami.

“I do what my board tells me to 

do. And the board has not told me 
to take a position. And I don’t 
think that will change as there are 
members of IATA on all sides of 
the debate,” says Tyler.

He adds that while there could 

be “a robust debate on the ﬂoor of 
the hall” which “may be a great 
sport for people to see”, the issue 
is outside the scope of IATA’s ac-
tivities and any discussions here 
are unlikely “to move the debate 
forward very far”.

Of late, IATA AGMs have earned 

a reputation for throwing up a few 
surprises from the ﬂoor. But Tyler 
welcomes his members taking an 
active part in the AGM: “It’s ﬁne for 
these things to come out of the 
blue. If someone wants to criticise 
what we’re doing or what we’re not 
doing, let’s hear it.”

Emphasising that IATA is the 

airlines’ “servant, not their lead-
er”, he adds: “We’re here to act for 
the industry as a means to an end, 
not an end in itself.”

US Airways frequent ﬂiers to the

It implemented a system-wide 
codeshare and moved US Air-
ways to Oneworld from Star Alli-

But one of the most signiﬁcant 

integration events of any airline 
merger remains ahead – consoli-
dation to a single reservations sys-

we have a lot left and we’re by no 
means done,” said Parker in an in-

SINGLE SYSTEM

American plans to begin a “drain 
down” of the US Airways reserva-
tions system as early as July, with 
the cutover to a single system oc-
curring 90 days later – or as early 
as October. The clock starts when 
the airline stops taking new reser-
vations on the US Airways sys-
tem, allowing them to slowly 
drain the reservations out and 
leaving only about 10% of the res-
ervations in the smaller carrier’s 
system when the cutover occurs.

The carrier hopes to avoid some 

of the issues that affected thou-
sands of passengers during prior 

airline mergers – including United 
Airlines and Continental Airlines 
in 2012 and US Airways and 
America West Airlines under 
Parker’s lead in 2007 – with the 
drain down approach.

Despite the remaining integra-

tion hurdles, Parker is conﬁdent 
in the success of the merger.

 “Shortly before the merger I 

was asked by the board ‘what 
keeps you up at night, what wor-
ries you?’ and I said if we have any 
sort of speed bump early on,” he 
says, explaining that he worries 
such a bump could cause the 
American and US Airways work-
forces to split before they ever re-
ally came together.

This occurred between US Air-

ways east – legacy US Airways – 
and west – legacy America West – 
pilots following their 2005 merger. 
The integration hit a number of 
snags, including an integrated 
pilot seniority list that east pilots 
felt slighted by, resulting in the 
carrier operating with two sepa-
rate and distinct pilot groups 
through to today.

 “If we hit a speed bump [now] 

– and we don’t intend to – I know 
the team wouldn’t fracture,” says 
Parker. This cohesion, regardless 
of what happens over the next six 
months, symbolises the successful 
internal integration of the airlines.

American still has to meet the 

external targets that it has set for 
itself. These include a relatively 
smooth move to a single reserva-
tions system, but also achieving 
the at-least $1.4 billion in revenue 
synergies executives outlined to 
investors early in the integration.

REMAINING BENEFITS

The airline has already achieved 
about one-third of these synergies, 
but can only realise the remaining 
beneﬁts once the integration is 
complete. If these come to pass, the 
carrier’s already record quarterly 
earnings could get even better. 
American reported an operating 
proﬁt of $1.22 billion on operating 
revenues of $9.83 billion in the ﬁrst 
quarter. Net proﬁt was $932 mil-
lion after special items and its pre-
tax margin was 12.7%.

“The things that we’re doing to 

produce the results we have will 
continue,” says Parker. “Setting 
aside what happens to the global 
economy and fuel prices, we don’t 
have the two airlines integrated. 
Getting off the US Airways code-
share to one American Airlines, 
we’ll be able to do a lot more with 
the integrated network and that 

will help our performance.” Net-
work changes include spooling 
each of American’s nine hubs to 
their strengths and increasing 
equipment swaps – for example 
shifting some Airbus A319s out of 
legacy US Airways hubs to legacy 
American ones, and Boeing 
 737-800s in the other direction. 
Other efﬁciencies include moving 
to single from multiple systems.

Some of the network changes 

include expanding internationally 
where American is weak – namely 
to Asia. 

Capacity growth across the Pa-

ciﬁc will be higher than the 2% to 
3% international average at the 
airline this year, says Parker.

This includes the new Beijing-

Dallas/Fort Worth service that 
began in May and lapping the 
joint launches of Hong Kong-Dal-
las/Fort Worth and Shanghai-Dal-
las/Fort Worth in June 2014. 

Parker is keeping quiet on when 

the carrier will announce its next 
new route to Asia, but previous 
statements indicate that such an 
announcement from its gateway at 
Los Angeles International could 
occur before the end of the year.

FOCUS

IATA’s hesitation to take sides is 

unsurprising, given that several 
IATA members have positioned 
themselves on either side of the 
divide as the row deepened.

United’s Star Alliance ally, Luf-

thansa Group, has offered its sup-
port to the US carriers. The labour 
unions of United, Delta and Amer-
ican are ﬁrmly backing the airlines 
in their campaign, and so is the 
pilot union of Southwest Airlines, 
though the airline has so far not 
taken a position on the issue. 

The US mainline carriers’ con-

cerns have also been echoed by 
US politicians, smaller US air-
ports as well as domestic trade as-
sociations.

GULF BACKING

On the Gulf side, IAG recently 
voiced its support for partner and 
shareholder Qatar Airways. The 
difference in opinions among Eu-
rope’s major carriers has led to a 
rift within the Association of Eu-
ropean Airlines. IAG carriers Brit-
ish Airways and Iberia withdrew 
from the association in April, fol-
lowed by the exit of Air Berlin and 
Alitalia – which both count Eti-
had as their largest shareholder.

Among other US airlines, Jet-

Blue Airways and FedEx Express 
have been staunch supporters of 
the Gulf carriers. JetBlue code-
shares with all three Gulf airlines 
and its chief executive Robin 
Hayes has been vocal in calling on 
the US government to disregard 
the US mainline carriers’ claims.

The US mainline carriers are so 

adamant in the demand for their 
government to take action that 
their chief executives appeared 
jointly at the National Press Club 
in May in a rare display of unity.

“I hate these guys, let me tell 

you – and they hate me too – [but] 
we’re here together because this is 
such a serious issue,” said United 
chief executive Jeff Smisek, sitting 
alongside Delta’s Richard Ander-
son and American’s Doug Parker.

Two months before the Press 

Club event, the US majors ﬁred the 
ﬁrst salvo by unveiling a white 
paper stating the alleged subsidies 
received by the Gulf carriers. 

A month later, the US airlines 

released detailed ﬁnancial docu-
ments that they say back up their 
claims in the white paper. Running 
more than 1,000 pages long, the 
documents were pieced together 
by trawling through ﬁnancial state-
ments ﬁled by the Gulf carriers in 
several jurisdictions.

Rumours of the white paper had 

circulated in Washington DC for 
months prior to its ofﬁcial release 
to the public in March, with the US 
mainline carriers peddling the re-
port privately to senior US ofﬁcials 
and certain media outlets. This had 
the Gulf carriers calling foul.

All three Gulf carriers have 

strongly maintained that they do 
not receive subsidies. Etihad has 
released its own research and 
studies, ﬁring back at the US 
mainline carriers. One of these re-
ports, commissioned by Etihad, 
alleges that the US airlines had 
beneﬁtted from more than $70 bil-
lion in government support. The 
bulk of this is related to beneﬁts as 
a result of the Chapter 11 bank-

ruptcy protection in the USA, 
which all three US mainline carri-
ers have gone through.

Etihad’s general counsel and 

company secretary Jim Callaghan 
has said the airline does not wish 
to question the “legitimacy of ben-
eﬁts” provided to US carriers by 
their government and bankruptcy 
courts. “We simply wish to high-
light the fact that US carriers have 
been beneﬁtting and continue to 
beneﬁt from a highly favourable 
legal regime,” he says, adding that 
these beneﬁts are “generally only 
available to US carriers”.

The US mainline carriers, how-

ever, do not agree. Indeed, most of 
the arguments between both sides 
have revolved around what consti-
tutes a subsidy and what does not. 

DEFINING MOMENT

Delta, American and United say 
their accusations are based on a 
globally accepted deﬁnition of 
subsidy by the WTO. Their Gulf 
rivals have rejected this, with 

 

Qatar’s Al Baker telling CNN in a 
February interview: “Quite frank-
ly, I think Mr Richard Anderson 
needs to go and study in a univer-
sity to ﬁnd out what the difference 
is between equity and subsidy.”

US authorities have so far not 

indicated what action, if any, they 
will take on the US carriers’ com-
plaint aside from stating that they 
are beginning a review. “We’re try-

ing to balance the need for a 
prompt government decision on 
how to respond to the legacies 
with the need to do a good job,” 
said Thomas Engle, deputy assis-
tant secretary for transportation 
affairs at the US Department of 
State, in May. 

In the meantime, however, the 

US government has not stopped 
the Gulf carriers from adding new 
capacity to the USA. Emirates and 
Qatar have both announced new 
US routes, to the chagrin of the US 
mainline carriers.

Adding an interesting angle to 

the dispute is the relationship be-
tween American and Qatar, which 
now make strange bedfellows in 
the Oneworld alliance as they bat-
tle over the subsidy allegations. 
The two carriers have reiterated 
their commitment to each other, 
characterising the partnership as 
“commercial” and separate from 
the subsidies spat. 

“My dear friend Doug Parker 

has been misled by an individual. 
When he sits across from me at the 
table, he will realise that absolute-
ly there is no issue for him,” said 
Al Baker of a potential meeting 
with Parker at the IATA AGM.

While industry watchers wait 

for the next move by the US gov-
ernment, airline executives will 
likely ﬁnd themselves in a highly 
charged gathering in Miami – 
whether IATA likes it or not.

Here it appears there is a

mentality has been inﬂuenced by

better to ﬁnd some stability”.

“Governments in Europe, one way 
or the other, have got their hands 
full with the economy, health, ed-
ucation and pensions, so they 
can’t really take any more on,” he 
says, noting that, as a result, re-

sources cannot be directed into 
more marginal things like airlines.

Morris also sees the experience 

of markets being sustained when a 
carrier has fallen as an inﬂuential

was when you had a carrier stag-

Malev experience shows things

airline safety, while also highlighting security’s importance in managing risk and reassuring passengers

 L

ed public perceptions of

dental? They can be just as fatal. 
As IATA director general Tony 
Tyler recently remarked: “Despite 
the improving safety trend, we 
have been confronted by a se-
quence of what seem like random 
disasters that have raised ques-
tions. Three in particular have 
grabbed the global attention of 
the media and regulators.”

HEADLINE NEWS

The Ascend 2014 Safety Percep-

global’s data and consultancy arm, 
starts by quoting a newspaper 
headline fairly representative of 
media reaction: “As another jet 
crashes… is it safe to ﬂy?” The

will be remembered for the loss of

niﬁcant impact on the public per-

The fact is that passengers died 

in these aircraft. Nervous travellers 
do not distinguish between the 

causes of death. And as the fre-
quency of crashes caused by tech-
nical faults continues to fall, the 
proportion of those caused by 
human factors and illegal interfer-
ence will increase in relative terms.

However caused, these losses 

are, for headline writers, “crash-
es”. Now that the Germanwings 
loss, according to the investigator, 
appears to have been caused by 
the co-pilot’s deliberate suicidal 
action alone in the cockpit, are the 
accident statisticians going to be 
able to claim any relevance for ﬁg-
ures that take no account of it?

2014 was the best 12 months ever 
for airline safety, according to As-
cend, despite the losses of the two 
MAS 777s and an AirAsia A320 
just before year-end.

and insurance, Paul Hayes, reveals 
that the global airline fatal accident

basis 2014 was, narrowly, the saf-

the 17 July loss over eastern 
Ukraine of MAS ﬂight MH17, on 
the grounds that it was shot down 
and is therefore considered a war 
risk loss, not an accident. But al-
though doubts exist about the sta-

tus of missing MAS ﬂight MH370, 
that tragedy has been included in 
the fatal accident rates. If the disap-
pearance were, however, eventual-
ly conﬁrmed as the result of a de-
liberate act by someone on board 
– as many experts in Malaysia and 
elsewhere now believe – and if it 
were therefore excluded from the 
accident statistics, its absence 
would make the 2014 ﬁgures ap-
pear even more impressive.

cidents we cannot rely on their in-
vestigation alone to guide our ef-
forts. Alone they cannot yield the 
trend data that is vital to a system-

data from all ﬂights, not just the 
inﬁnitesimal percentage of ﬂights

from 2014 – delivered by the fate of 
ﬂights MH370 and MH17 – is that 
security is as important as opera-
tional and engineering safety in

commission, and IATA, is looking 
at the risks of ﬂying over or close to 
conﬂict zones as MH17 did, but de-
termining what to do about a mys-
tery like MH370 or a tragedy caused 
by extreme human factors like Ger-
manwings, is more difﬁcult.

OVERSEEING PRICES

In the early days, governments in-
sisted on the right to oversee the 
prices charged by international 
airlines but could not, in practical 
terms, develop those prices for 
themselves. IATA was delegated 
to hold trafﬁc conferences for this 
purpose, with all fares and rates 

subject to ﬁnal government ap-
proval. The aim was two-fold: en-
suring that fares and rates would 
not involve cut-throat competi-
tion, while making sure they 
could be set as low as possible, in 
the interests of consumers. 

A coherent pattern of fares and 

rates was established, avoiding in-
consistencies between tariffs af-
fecting neighbouring countries – and 
thereby avoiding trafﬁc diversion. 
The predictability of fares and rates 
in this pattern also enabled air-
lines to accept each others’ tickets 
on multi-sector journeys and thus 
gave birth to inter lining. Today, 50 
million international air passengers 
a year pay for their ticket in one 
place, in one currency, but com-
plete their journey using at least 
two (and sometimes ﬁve or more) 

airlines from different countries 
using different currencies.

In 1994 the association rede-

ﬁned its mission and goals into 

the following six categories: safety 
and security; industry recogni-
tion; ﬁnancial viability; products 
and services; standards and proce-
dures; and industry support. 

The past decade has been a tu-

multuous one for the airline in-
dustry, featuring crises such as 

terrorism, pandemic threats, vol-
canic eruptions, global economic 
upheavals and fuel price hikes. In 
a bid to address these issues, when 
Bisignani took the helm at IATA in 
2002 he initiated a complete re-
structuring of the association.

When Tyler succeeded Bisignani 

in 2011, he put an immediate 
focus on creating partnerships 
across the industry. He continues 
to call on governments to intro-
duce better regulation, improved 
infrastructure and favourable tax 
regimes to enable the air transport 
sector to continue growing.

A series of celebrations will 

take place across the 50 IATA of-
ﬁces around the globe throughout 
the course of this year, and this 
year’s AGM will also be used to 
mark the 70-year anniversary.

proximity to Cuba means that no 
visit is complete without a trip to 
the 

Little Havana district

.

Visitors in search of good, old-

fashioned American comfort food 
– think cheeseburgers, ribs and 
pot roast – could do much worse 
than to head for 

Blue Collar

 on Bis-

cayne Boulevard. The restaurant 
prides itself on serving “food that 
people like to eat” in a casual and 
friendly setting. Aside from the 
aforementioned crowd-pleasers, 
Blue Collar also offers some 
tempting seafood dishes, includ-
ing crispy skin snapper and 
seared diver scallops, along with a 
varied list of vegetarian options.

Miami is located just over 

320km (200 miles) from Cuba and 
is home to a large Cuban immi-
grant population, meaning there is 
no shortage of restaurants serving 
up fare from the island nation.

One such renowned establish-

ment is 

Exquisito Restaurant

 on 

Calle Ocho – Little Havana’s main 
drag. Exquisito has picked up nu-
merous accolades throughout its 
30-year history, particularly for 
menu mainstays such as the 
Cuban sandwich, vaca frita 
(shredded beef) and tamales. The 
restaurant is also considered good 

value for money, with most menu 
items priced at or below $15.

While you’re in Little Havana, 

take some time to explore its 
Cuban heritage and architecture. 
Soak up the atmosphere and im-
agine you’re in the real Cuban 
capital as you walk past elderly 
men playing dominoes on street 
corners and savour the heady scent 
of cigar smoke. History buffs may 
also enjoy a visit to the 

Bay of Pigs 

Museum

, which commemorates 

the failed military invasion of Cuba 
by the CIA-sponsored paramilitary 
group Brigade 2506 in 1961. The 
invasion was an attempt to over-
throw the Communist government 
of Fidel Castro, but it ultimately 
led to the deaths of over 100 of the 
attackers and the imprisonment of 
more than 1,000 more – not to 
mention huge embarrassment for 
the US government.

Another Cuban restaurant locat-

ed away from Little Havana, and 
one that is famed for its mojitos, is 

Larios on the Beach

 on Ocean 

Drive. This restaurant also has an 
element of celebrity glitz and glam-
our as it is co-owned by Cuban-
American singer-songwriter Gloria 
Estefan (remember the Miami 
Sound Machine?). Larios serves up 
a combination of contemporary 
and traditional Cuban dishes, in 

addition to a list of “Triple C” – or 
“Calorie Conscious Cuban” – op-
tions devised by Gloria herself. 

To wash this down, be sure to 

order a mojito from the restau-
rant’s extensive cocktail list. Fla-
vour options include traditional, 
guava, strawberry, raspberry, wa-
termelon, pineapple and mango. 
Or if you’d rather sample a 

 

cocktail designed by Ms Estefan, 
try her “Glorious Cosmopolitan” 
– a slight variation on the drink 
made famous by Sex and the City.

If you’re feeling Italian, 

Osteria 

del Teatro

 in South Beach’s Art 

Deco district gets rave reviews. 
This restaurant has been serving 
up homemade pasta and other 
Italian staples for the last 25 years. 
A daily specials board also fea-
tures a mouth-watering list of pre-
dominantly seafood dishes, such 
as lobster with pappardelle and 
grilled jumbo shrimp.

enjoy strolling around the 

Art

its preservation.
