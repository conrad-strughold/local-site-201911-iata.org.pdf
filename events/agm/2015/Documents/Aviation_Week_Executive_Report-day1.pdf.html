U.S. DOT Chief’s Keynote 

Could Address Gulf Dispute

U.S. Department of  Transportation (DOT) 

SecretaryAnthony Foxx will deliver a keynote 

address during the opening session of  the 

International Air Transport Association Annual 

General Meeting (IATA AGM)in Miami Monday, 

raising the possibility that the ongoing dispute 

between the three major U.S. carriers and the 

three largest Gulf  airlines could be discussed, 

despite it not being on the AGM agenda.

The U.S. government is reviewing claims by American, 

Delta and United airlines that Emirates Airline, Etihad 

Airways and Qatar Airways have been heavily subsidized 

by the governments of the United Arab Emirates (UAE) 

and Qatar, which own them, and that these alleged subsidies contravene those countries’ open skies 

agreements with the U.S. The Gulf carriers strongly deny the subsidy claims and say U.S. carriers and 

those they merged with have saved billions of dollars through U.S. Chapter 11 bankruptcy restructuring.

Deadline for submitting comments on the issue to the U.S. Commerce, State and Transporta-

tion Departments closed at the end of  May. It is not yet known what will happen next, or whether 

government-to-government talks will be held as requested by the U.S. carriers.The agenda for the 

71st IATA AGM does not include any panels or discussion on the topic. Although the CEOs of  all 

six carriers involved in the dispute are expected to attend, none of  the U.S. CEOs are scheduled 

to speak. Etihad President and CEO James Hogan is listed to join the opening day’s CEO panel 

this afternoon with the CEOs of  Alaska Airlines, Lufthansa, Malaysia Airlines and TAM Airlines.

American Airlines is a co-host of  the event, along with FedEx Express, UPS and U.S.-airline lob-

bying association Airlines for America (A4A). FedEx and UPS are against the U.S. airline campaign, 

fearing a roll back of  U.S. open skies policy. Some smaller U.S. carriers, notably JetBlue Airways and 

Alaska, are also concerned about potential negative effects of  the U.S. campaign. A4A meanwhile, 

has members on both sides of  the debate and has remained neutral.

IATA, which is bound to represent all of  its members, has similarly stayed neutral on the Gulf  

dispute. Some CEOs that ATW spoke with on the eve of  the AGM said the rumor was that Qatar 

Airways Group CEO Akbar Al Baker was planning to raise the dispute. Qatar Airways, a five-

star carrier, is the only one of  the major Gulf  carriers that belongs to a global alliance, oneworld. 

Qatar also owns a 10% stake in British Airways (BA). Willie Walsh, CEO of  International Airlines 

Group (IAG), which owns BA, has stood almost alone among European flag carrier executives in 

challenging U.S. carriers’ motives for their anti-Gulf-airline campaign.

Up to 1,000 delegates are expected to attend the AGM, the largest gathering of  airline CEOs 

and senior executives. Other topics scheduled for discussion include infrastructure investment, how 

to make airlines “investible,” dramatic structural changes in the air-cargo business; and passenger 

trends that airlines need to note in their customer-loyalty programs.

Karen Walker/Air Transport World

IATA To Hold 2016 AGM 
In Dublin

The International Air Transport Association 

Annual General Meeting (IATA AGM) will 

take place in Dublin next year, the Aviation 

Week Network has learned, marking a return 

to a European city after six years. 

The last AGM in Europe took place in Ber-

lin in 2010, and has since been held in Asia 

(Beijing, Singapore), Africa (Cape Town), and 

North America (Miami). Dublin is the base for 

IATA member Aer Lingus—which is about to be 

bought by International Airlines Group (IAG)—

and a carrier currently the source of trouble for 

Europe’s network airlines: Ryanair. IATA plans 

to announce the location of next year’s gathering 

at this year’s closing session on June 9.

on the eve of the annual general meeting.

Müller Predicts Fast 
Consolidation In Asia

Malaysia Airlines CEO Christoph Mül-

ler believes industry consolidation will 

happen much quicker in Asia than it has 

in Europe and the U.S.

Müller told Aviation Week at the International Air 

Transport Association Annual General Meeting (IATA 

AGM) in Miami that he expects major progress in that 

regard within the next five years, as countries continue 

to liberalize their air service agreements and allow their 

national carriers to combine forces.

Müller took over as acting CEO of Malay-

sia Airlines in May. The airline’s assets are to be 

transferred to a new company, Malaysia Airlines 

Berhard, which is launching on Sept. 1. The com-

pany will have a group structure, including 12 

subsidiaries for units such as catering or mainte-

nance. This structure will allow the airline to take 

an equity partner or sell stakes in individual units. 

But Müller says this strategy is more of a long-

term scenario that would only likely become real-

ity after the new Malaysia Airlines is profitable.

Müller, who headed Aer Lingus until earlier 

this year, has a mandate to make the airline prof-

itable within three years. Around 6,000 of the 

current 20,000 employees will not transfer to the 

new airline, but Müller says that number could 

still change. “There is a safety net built into the 

6,000” that accounts for uncertainties such as 

the value of the U.S. dollar or the fuel prices, he 

says. The airline has extended some contracts on 

a temporary basis, but could make them perma-

nent if it performs better than expected.

Pilots have been offered new contracts based 

on a benchmark study among seven airlines in 

the region aimed at finding the going market 

rate. Some pilots will see a slight pay reduction, 

and others will get raises, Müller explains. But 

all of  them will lose prior seniority.

Müller sees MAS becoming a “value car-

rier” that will offer a basic product and optional 

high-end services. One of the key enablers of  

transformation will be a new information tech-

nology infrastructure that will allow the airline 

to offer more products and enhance back-office 

functions and processes, such as maintenance 

planning. “We will likely chose the revolution-

ary approach,” he said, and not try to adapt the 

current systems to new standards.

Deferred Pain for IATA CEO

Tony Tyler, IATA’s director deneral and CEO, could be looking 

ahead at a relatively relaxed Annual General Assembly (AGM). 

The location in Miami Beach is nice, there are no major deci-

sions to be taken by the board, the industry is doing reasonably 

well. And the one big policy issue that the industry will have 

to deal with—the introduction of  a global emissions trading 

scheme somehow organized by the International Civil Aviation 

Organization (ICAO)–is only really on the agenda for next year. 

In other words: “It is business as usual,” Tyler says.

But there are a few issues that have made it on the agenda at least 

informally: 2014 was the safest year in aviation by most parameters, but 

it will still be remembered for a long time because of  two highly unusual 

aircraft crashes: the disappearance of  flight MH370 and the shoot-down 

of  MH17 only a few months later. In 2015 the good overall safety trend 

continued, but then came the shock caused by 4U4595, the German-

wings flight that was apparently intentionally piloted into a mountain 

by the first officer. And of  course Miami will be yet another occasion to 

continue the increasingly bitter debate about Gulf  carriers, open skies 

and alleged subsidies.

A tragedy such as Germanwings was considered by many inside and 

outside the industry as unthinkable. But Tyler points out that in addi-

tion to the emerging discussion about more in-depth pilot checks, the 

case raises another issue: the immediate availability  of  aircraft accident 

information to the general public. “How can we deal with the demand 

for  instant information?” Tyler asks. He recognizes that “we are in a 

new era of  how these tragedies get into the public domain.” However, 

he warns that regulators, investigators and politicians have to make sure 

that the accident investigation process “is not jeopardized.”

The case of  Germanwings was a particularly stark reminder because 

it took place in France. Aircraft crashes there are pursued in a dual 

process—a criminal investigation led by a prosecutor and the regular 

BEA accident investigation. The prosecutor calls the shots and has 

access to all data and information that BEA uncovers. He can also 

release information about the case whenever he wishes. And in the 

case of  Germanwings, the prosecutor did: the world learned three days 

after the crash most of  what happened. “The prosecutor took control 

of  the events,” Tyler says.

NEUTRALITY ON SUBSIDY ISSUE

Germanwings may have been extreme, but other accidents have also 

been exposed to much more immediate scrutiny: After a Transasia ATR 

was filmed crashing into a highway and a river in Taiwan, social media 

channels were immediately full of  the footage. “Those things never used 

to happen,” Tyler says. But the IATA CEO accepts that the industry has 

to deal with the new reality. “We have to keep the elements of  success 

[in accident investigations] while recognizing the demand for a faster 

response.”

The one danger Tyler points at is the immediate public pressure on 

politicians to act, based on bits of  information that become available one 

after the other. Elements of  this wereseen after the Germanwings crash, 

too: several airlines introduced a rule whereby no pilot can ever be alone 

in the cockpit during flight.

The other debate is one in which IATA cannot really side with one 

party—the dispute about Gulf  carriers and alleged subsidies. “All of  

the airlines involved are key and very supportive members of  IATA,” 

Tyler says. And: “There are [a] lot of  common interests like safety, the

U.S. DOT Chief, Others Now Have Open Mind On FAA Restructuring

SELF-FUNDING PROGRAMS

NEW REVENUE STREAMS

COMPETITIVE MARKET INTELLIGENCE

ELIMINATE CREDIT CARD FEES

The notion of  a restructured FAA that could include taking 

air traffic control out of  the agency is starting to gain traction 

in Washington, with U.S. Transportation Secretary Anthony 

Foxx among those willing to have an open mind on the issue.

U.S. airlines have endorsed “a commercialized, nonprofit-type gover-

nance structure” for ATC, though they are a bit wary of  the term “priva-

tization.” House of  Representatives Transportation and Infrastructure 

Committee Chairman Bill Shuster (R-Pa.) has said “the only answer” to 

fixing air traffic management in the U.S. “is transformational reform that 

will ensure that our ATC service provider operates like a business.” Even 

National Air Traffic Controllers Association (NATCA) union President 

Paul Rinaldi has called NAV Canada—the private, nonprofit entity that 

runs Canadian ATC—“very intriguing.”

Foxx is choosing his words on the issue carefully, telling ATW that the 

U.S. has the “gold standard air traffic system in the world,” and that he 

is “particularly proud of  our air traffic controllers.”

But he added, “We will be open to thinking about different structures, 

but the pivot point for looking at new structures is to recognize that we 

have the safest system in the world, and we don’t want to jeopardize that.”

All of  this talk about ATC partial privatization and revamping FAA 

comes ahead of  the FAA-reauthorization bill Congress will take up later 

this year—FAA’s current authorization expires Sept. 30. Shuster has said 

he wants the bill to be “big and bold.”

The question now is how quickly a concrete proposal on ATC reform 

can be put forward, and whether enough players can be convinced to back 

it. It’s easy to be “open” when there’s nothing tangible to be for or against.

“There are a lot of  ideas floating around and we’re looking to work 

with Congress on this,” Foxx said. “The focus should be on making the 

system better.”

Managing ATC has always been an odd fit for FAA. It makes the 

agency a key service provider for a commercial air transport system that 

it also regulates, creating an inevitable conflict of  interest. It also means 

ATC gets roped into the annual Congressional appropriations process, 

making it difficult, for example, to get a steady, long-term financial com-

mitment to deploying the satellite-based NextGen ATC system. “Many 

stakeholders have understandably lost confidence in FAA’s ability to 

modernize,” Shuster has pointed out.

At an Aero Club of  Washington appearance last year, Foxx said, “I 

know there’s a lot of  concern that the political underpinnings of  our 

aviation system may be fraying. I’m willing to keep my ears and eyes 

open to new ways of  doing things.”

Will this be the year of  a genuine, radical restructuring of  FAA that 

creates a separate NAV Canada-like entity to manage ATC? The debate 

will begin in earnest soon, likely as the overwhelming aviation-policy issue 

on Capitol Hill this summer. 

Aaron Karp/Air Transport World

For years the International Air Transport Association (IATA)

In France, for example, discussions are ongoing between Aeroports

There is an additional difference of  opinion: While ADP calculates 

airport charges using the dual-till system, airlines prefer the single-till 

method. Dual till separates regulated-business charges—such as those 

for air bridges and landing and parking aircraft—from non-regulated 

business charges, such as those for retail, office space and car parkings. 

Dual till then sets a price cap for regulated-business charges. But single 

till deducts non-regulated businesses' net revenue from total regulated 

revenue, thereby including non-aernautical profits in the regulatory 

till. The single-till system thus allows airlines to benefit more from 

passengers' spending.ADP calculates airport charges using a “dual-till”

Airline Planning 100 Years  
In Advance

Can airlines learn from a Norwegian library, which just has 

embarked on a project not to be completed for another 100 years?

Just outside Oslo, 1,000 trees have been planted. Once grown, these 

trees will be used for paper. On that paper, 100 stories will be published, 

100 years from now. One-hundred writers, selected to create a collection 

of  100 stories over 100 years, which will only be read in 2114: This is 

the Future Library.

I read about this project in Emirates’ inflight magazine, traveling from 

London Gatwick to Dubai. “Every decision I make now regarding Future 

Library, I have to think of  in 100-year time spans,” Katie Paterson, the 

Scottish artist behind Future Library, says in the article.

“How will the library room be looked at and experienced in 100 years? 

How will the materials react over the decades to come? What languages 

will people be speaking in 100 years? What kind of  technologies will exist? 

What will the status of  the printed book be?” she asks. 

This made me think about airline planning and how hard it is to 

conceive what the airline industry will look like in 2114.

It also reminded me of a conversation I had with Ethiopian Airlines CEO 

Tewolde Gebremariam in Johannesburg in 2012, when we were talking about 

his airline’s strategic plan to 2025. “It is difficult to plan for the next quarter, 

let alone the next 15 years,” he joked. Yet, despite this, Ethiopian has a clear 

“Vision 2025” plan stretching beyond the next decade.

JUNE 8, 2015

Gulf Carrier Dispute Will  
Slow Down Liberalization

As the world’s airlines meet in Miami for the International Air Transport Associa-

tion’s (IATA) Annual General Meeting, one of  the most pressing policy issues will 

not be on the official agenda: how to deal with Gulf  carriers and, more broadly, gov

-

ernment support in the industry. But the dispute has a major political effect beyond 

this case: further liberalization, much demanded by many, is becoming less likely.

The Gulf  carrier issue is not on the IATA meeting agenda for a simple reason of  protocol: 

The association represents airlines from both increasingly hostile camps and cannot take a stance 

without hurting the interests of  important members. So it does not.

When the U.S. coalition of  airlines and unions released their study into the alleged $40 billion-

plus in subsidies they claim Qatar Airways, Etihad and Emirates have received, it argued this sup-

port was just the “tip of  the iceberg.” This holds true in ways that the coalition did not necessarily 

mean: Any discussion about state backing for particular airlines, and how to deal with the issue, 

raises topics such as the future of  bilateral air service agreements, foreign ownership and control 

reform, and the roadblocks to industry consolidation.

The clashes have made further liberalization much more difficult to achieve; the global air 

transport industry is now “unlikely to see any acceleration in liberalization,” International Airlines 

Group (IAG) CEO Willie Walsh predicts, IAG is one of  the few major players in Europe or the 

United States to see open markets as an opportunity, rather than a threat. Its only companion in 

the immediate vicinity is a new competitor in the London long-haul market: Norwegian. The car-

rier has unsuccessfully tried to get a U.S.-flying permit for its Ireland-based subsidiary, Norwegian 

Air International (NAI).

FIFTH-FREEDOM FEARS

Walsh is also one of  the few industry executives to state the motivation behind initiatives 

against Gulf  carriers or Norwegian—that U.S. airlines are trying to keep Emirates, Etihad and 

Qatar Airways from growing further to protect their own favorable profit margins. Meanwhile, 

Europeans are concerned their already-weak performance could worsen shouldcompetitors make 

more inroads into their markets.

In his observation, the availability of  some fifth-freedom rights from Europe to the U.S. has 

been an important element for the launch of  the campaign against the big three Gulf  carriers, 

as U.S. airlines are concerned they could become more direct competitors. The Milan-New 

York route operated by Emirates has been highlighted in particular. However, while “they have a 

number of  [fifth-freedom] rights they are not using, we don’t see that as a particular issue for us.”

Since then, however, Emirates Airline President Tim Clark has indicated his carrier might 

soon utilize more of  those rights, given the campaign it is facing.

Liberalization has been an ideal in the industry since the 1978 deregulation of  the U.S. market. 

The EU later took the concept one step further by eliminating regulatory barriers inside the EU, 

thus not limiting the more liberal approach to one country, but to an entire region. The 2008 

U.S-EU open skies agreement was another landmark for consolidation as well as liberalization, 

as it denoted acceptance of  EU ownership for what was still in essence a bilateral air service 

agreement.

CHANGING LEGAL FRAMEWORK

But fast forward fewer than 10 years, and the limits of  the old bilateral and ownership and 

control system burdening most airlines are more than obvious. The burdens seem to grow heavier 

every day. The irony is that tactical consider-

ations will likely lead to keeping the system 

unchanged for now.

European airlines in particular have long 

paid lip-service to getting rid of  what they 

described as outdated regulation. That was in 

the days when they considered themselves to 

be the acquirers of  airlines in other parts of  

the world. Today, the  legal framework within 

which the airlines operate has–in a different 

context–returned to being a protective mecha-

nism. The same is true for bilateral air service 

agreements, which now serve as the basis for 

complaints against the presence of  Gulf  car-

riers in the U.S. and Europe.

As for ownership and control, the number 

of  European airlines that would like to see 

that concept replaced by regulation based 

on “principle place of  business” is growing 

by the day. Carriers such as Air Berlin and 

Alitalia are dependent upon financial sup

-

port from non-EU investors, Etihad in their 

case. A number of  smaller European airlines 

have been up for sale–TAP Portugal or Air 

Baltic to name but two–and have found no 

investors.

Jens Flottau/Aviation Week & Space Technology
