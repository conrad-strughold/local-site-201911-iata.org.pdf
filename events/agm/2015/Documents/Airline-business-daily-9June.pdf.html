Director general Tony Tyler is playing 
guinea pig for an initiative involving 
wearable technology that links with  
the SkyZen app designed to reduce the 
effect of jet lag.

“It’s a very new idea that we can 

learn a lot about how people sleep 
and rest on aeroplanes by looking at 
big data drawn through this device 
and the technology of apps and 
iPhones,” says Tyler. 

Tyler expects the technology, 

which uses the Jawbone fitness 
wristband, to help understand 
ways to better manage jet lag. 
“Knowledge comes from informa-
tion so when we collect the  
information we will know  
what we can find out about  
it,” he says.

AG chief Willie Walsh, speaking 
in a panel discussion yesterday 

at the AGM, called on airlines, in 
partnership with IATA, to put the 
microscope on restrictive practices 
in the maintenance world.

“As an industry we need to start 

taking action or our maintenance 
costs will definitely rise,” says 
Walsh. “If we don’t challenge the 
restrictive practices that exist, we 

will be held captive and costs will 
rise and rise.

“We have to start redressing this 

situation. The IATA board of gover-
nors was unanimous on the issue, 
and they are going to engage exter-
nal legal counsel to start looking 
into this issue,” he says. “We need 
to start pushing back on our costs 
where they are driven by a limited 
number of suppliers.”

Walsh says that there are anec-

dotes of dramatic rises in MRO 
costs as a result of carriers being 
forced to enter into restrictive con-
tracts for maintenance services and 
the perception amongst airlines is 
that the market is becoming more 
restrictive, not less. 

“This is something as an indus-

try we have to address. Our share-
holders expect it of us,” he says.

Emirates Airline president Sir 

Tim Clark expressed the issue an-
other way during the panel. 
“There’s no way we will let engine 
costs per flight hour rise on new 
generation jets. We expect unit costs 
to come down by as much as 20%, 
especially on power by the hour 
deals,” he says. Clark sees the new 
generation of engines making “huge 
advances” in unit cost reduction.

I

ATA lifted its collective profits 
forecast for 2015 to $29.3 bil-

lion, around half of which will be 
generated by North American car-
riers, as airlines benefit from 
lower fuel costs to the extent the 
industry is finally out-performing 
its cost of capital.

It marks an improvement on the 

$25 billion profit for 2015 IATA 
forecast in its last industry outlook 
in December. It builds on the re-

stated $16.4 billion airlines made 
last year and is the industry’s larg-
est ever profit in absolute terms.

“Many airlines are winning in 

the basic struggle to keep revenues 
ahead of costs. Enough that, for 
the first in IATA’s records, the in-
dustry as a whole is earning its 
cost of capital,” says director gen-
eral Tony Tyler.  

Lower fuel costs have driven 

this improvement, but non-US 

carriers are missing out on some of 
the gains because of extra costs  
from the strength of the US dollar.

North American carriers are 

now expected  to generate net 
profits of $15.7 billion this year, 
accounting for over half the pro-
jected industry figure this year 
and almost matching last year’s 
total profit for the entire industry. 
This marks a $4.5 billion improve-
ment on 2014.

irline chiefs have taken issue

they are faced with accusations of

The topic came up for discus-

featured newly minted Malaysia

by the  loss of two Boeing 777s,

flight MH370.

Lufthansa chief executive 

Carsten Spohr says his airline is 
now tracking aircraft every 5min 
instead of 15min but was quick to 
lament. “We are spending too 
much money on this at this 
point,” he says.

Mueller, however, disagrees that 

the costs associated with aircraft 
tracking technology are prohibi-
tive. He says that the devices are 
“ready to be ordered” for less than 

$1,000, compared to an aircraft 
business class seat that can cost a 
lot more because such a seat is 
made to withstand “40gs” of force. 
“No one can survive 40gs... but the 
seat is still intact,” says Mueller.

Alaska Airlines chief executive 

Brad Tilden calls for quicker pro-
gress on an industry-wide solu-
tion to tackle the issue of aircraft 
tracking, pointing out that such 
technology in private aviation is 
relatively straightforward. 

“We need to move faster on 

this,” says Tilden.

Three years after it was first 
kicked off by IATA, the New 
Distribution Capability (NDC) ini-
tiative is gaining momentum with 
a host of airlines announcing pi-
lots and live deployments of the 
new standard here at the AGM.

T

wo engine choices on an air-
craft selection competition are 

always better than one, but the 
emergence of a third force to chal-
lenge Airbus and Boeing is not a 
given, even with China’s ambi-
tious plans in aircraft production.

These were two of the conclu-

sions of the Aircraft Manufacturers 
& Airlines: Partners? panel discus-
sion, moderated by Airline Busi-
ness editor Max Kingsley-Jones.

For Aengus Kelly, chief execu-

tive of leading lessor AerCap, it is 
“vital” customers have engine se-
lection choices when ordering air-
craft. “We cannot have a monopoly 
on the engine side,” he says. The 
big worry “is in the on-going cost of 
the maintenance if there’s a sole 
supplier”, he notes.

With the huge investment re-

quired to develop new engines, 
even a firm with the scale of GE 

needs to be selective. “I’d love to 
power every airplane in the market 
but we just can’t afford it. We pick 
and choose,” says David Joyce, 
president of GE Aviation.

“When you have a market 

which is large enough Airbus will 
try to offer two engines,” says 
Fabrice Bregier, president of Air-
bus. “We tried to do that on the 
A350 but we were not successful.”

Sir Tim Clark, president of Emir-

ates Airline, says that choice is nec-
essary not just for cost reasons but 
to ensure that competition spurs 
the right level of research and de-
velopment by the engine makers.

Choice for British Airways was 

important back in 2006 when it 
was choosing a 747-400 replace-
ment with the competition be-
tween the 747-8 and A380, says 
IAG head Willie Walsh. The new 
Boeing jumbo had a single choice 

of the GEnx engine while the A380 
had Rolls-Royce and Engine Alli-
ance to choose from. It was a situa-
tion where the 747-8 should have 
been a natural contender, says 
Walsh, but in the end BA chose the 
A380 with Trent engines.

No clear opinion on the prospect 

of China muscling in on the Airbus/ 
Boeing duopoly anytime soon 
emerged from the panellists. “If I 
was a customer, yes, I would want 
to have more choice,” says Bregier.

However, pricing remains ex-

tremely keen regardless of the ex-
istence of a third player. “From an 
Airbus perspective I can tell you 
competition with Boeing is very 
tough,” he says.

Kelly’s view is having two man-

ufacturers is “enough”. He says re-
gardless of the airline size “there 
will be a brutal battle between Boe-
ing and Airbus” for any order.

I

ATA continues to press Vene-
zuela’s government to repatriate 

billions in revenue owed to air-
lines, saying that the South Amer-
ican country’s severe economic 
situation is “not an excuse” for the 
hold-up.

Airlines are still owed about 

$3.7 billion in Venezuela in the 
long-running currency dispute – 
an amount that has not changed 
significantly in recent months.

“Our message is clear. This 

must stop,” says IATA director 
general and chief executive Tony 
Tyler. “This is airline money. It 
needs to be paid in full.”

IATA’s Caracas office has had 

“some success” in preventing the 
situation from worsening, notes 
Tyler. “Even the country’s severely 
difficult economic situation is not 
an excuse to delay addressing the 
issue,” he adds.

Tyler calls on Venezuela to end 

the use of different exchange rates 
and to work with airlines to agree 
on a payment plan, as well as to 
consult carriers before imposing 
new financial regulations.

Most airlines have opted to re-

duce service to the country, with 
several now accepting only US 
dollars in payment in Venezuela 
to avoid accumulating additional 
bolivares.

Mitsubishi MRJ

First flight of the Mitsubishi Aircraft MRJ regional jet 

has been pushed back to September or October 

2015, from the second quarter.

The Japanese manufacturer says the delay will not 

impact deliveries, which are due to begin in the 

second quarter of 2017.

 “Presently static strength testing and manufacture 

of the second and subsequent flight test aircraft are 

all proceeding smoothly,” says Mitsubishi. “In order 

to incorporate the verification results of the various 

ground tests into the first flight-test aircraft, the 

timing of the first flight has been reviewed.”

Subsequently, the manufacturer says it will conduct 

an “intensive” flight test campaign, and accelerate 

production at its Nagoya facility in order to meet the 

delivery timetable.

Mitsubishi previously announced a programme 

delay in August 2013. Then, it pushed its first flight 

from the third quarter of 2013 to the second 

quarter of 2015, and its first delivery from the 

summer of 2015 to the second quarter of 2017.

Boeing 737 Max

Development of Boeing’s re-engined 737 Max family 

has just passed two important milestones, with the 

twinjet’s powerplant entering flight-testing and 

assembly of the first aircraft beginning.

CFM International’s Leap-1B engine began flight-

testing on GE’s 747 flying test bed on 29 April at the 

company’s operations centre in Victorville, California.

The 5h 30min sortie launched a year-long 

programme to complete certification in 2016, and 

followed an 11-month series of ground testing that 

began last June.

Meanwhile the wing assigned to the first 737 Max 8 

is now taking shape at Boeing’s Renton plant near 

Seattle while Spirit AeroSystems has started to 

assemble structures for the first fuselage.

Boeing’s schedule calls for the first 737 Max 8 to fly 

next year, with deliveries to Southwest Airlines 

beginning in the third quarter of 2017. The US 

low-cost airline, which is launch operator for the new 

twinjet, holds firm orders for 170 737 Max 8s and 

30 Max 7s, along with an additional 191 options.
