IATA has signed a memorandum of understanding with the 
Mexican government to collaborate on the building of the 
new Mexico City airport.

The association’s regional vice-president for the 

Americas Peter Cerda calls the decision to build the new 
airport “welcome news”. He says: “We will be working 
with the government and shareholders to ensure that 
the airport meets the needs of airlines.” IATA will pro-
vide technical and operational assistance for the de-
sign and construction of the airport.

The new airport, however, will not be operational 

until 2020 and the current Mexico City airport is al-
ready at capacity, Cerda notes.

Airlines have long expressed concerns over the conges-

tion at Mexico City – mirroring sentiments over Latin 
America’s aviation infrastructure in general. Cerda re- 
iterates that Latin America’s air transport infrastructure still 
lags behind that in other parts of the world, and that not all 
airport privatisations in the region have been successful.

“Whatever actions are taken, capacity shortages won’t be 

solved overnight,” he says. “It is necessary to manage scarce 
runway resources.”

He cites countries like Singapore, China and those in the Gulf 

as examples that Latin American governments can look to when it 
comes to investing in aviation infrastructure. “Planning for any sort of 
infrastructure is not short-term,” says Cerda. “We as a region 
have not done a very good job.”

Cerda also reiterates IATA’s call for Latin 

American governments to recognise the 
value of the aviation industry as 
the provider of connectivity, 
instead of revenue.

made no secret of its ambitions to 
offer direct transatlantic links

“We are evaluating how to do it,” 
says Kondic. “It will not be a day 
sooner or a day earlier than it 
makes sense to.”

Air Serbia is leaning towards 

New York as the destination of 
most appeal in the USA, but is 
also looking at Chicago and To-
ronto, says Kondic.

However, whichever route to 

establishing US services it takes, 
the priority at the carrier is about 
achieving long-term success. “Our 
job is to bring it to life in the most 
responsible way to make it sus-
tainable,” he says.

M

ost Asian carriers have not 
been fazed by the debate rag-

ing in the USA over open skies 
with the Gulf carriers, says the 
head of the Association of Asia Pa-
cific Airlines Andrew Herdman.

He says the debate comes at a 

time when the likes of Emirates, 
Etihad Airways and Qatar Air-
ways are expanding in the USA. 
By comparison, they have already 
expanded into a raft of primary 
and secondary cities in Asia.

“From an Asian perspective 

we’ve had them in most of the 
markets including the secondary 
markets, with daily or multiple 
daily services,” Herdman says. 
“It’s a fact of life; they’re just part 
of the competition along with the 
other European, Asian and North 
American airlines.”

Instead, he would like to see the 

debate move towards changing 
the structures of bilateral air ser-
vices deals and other regulatory 

matters, as the industry “does op-
erate under some archaic rules.”

He notes that the move towards 

liberalisation is likely to continue, 
as passengers have seen the bene-
fits of more air services and greater 
competition. 

“I find it hard to imagine that 

the US government will reverse its 
policy on open skies, given it has 
been very much to the benefit of 
US carriers in particular,”  Herd-
man says.

The privately-owned Boeing 737 operator began Haj ser-

Europe must take a holistic approach to aviation 
regulation if it is ensure the continent can compete 
effectively with its peers on a global scale, believes 
Association of European Airlienes chief executive 
Athar Husain Khan.

It today submits its response to the European 

Commission’s consultation on its new aviation poli-
cy. “The key areas are: decrease the regulatory cost  
burden on industry; treat us as a global player – so 
don’t do anything in isolation in Europe and take a 
holistic view to issues; and create a level playing 
field within Europe and to the outside world.”

While the AEA is still reeling from the loss of sev-

eral key members, including British Airways/

Iberia parent IAG which defected to the 

European Low-Fare Airlines Association, 

Husain Khan sees an opportunity for 

the region’s disparate group of air-

line bodies to work together more 

effectively. “I regret that IAG left 

but at least there is a momen-

tum that we need to do some-

thing to make the 

airline-industry lobbying 

in Brussels more  

efficient and more 

effective,” he says.

Visitors coming through Miami

In the USA, where carriers are

The biggest beneficiary was 

American Airlines, which cut

resulting in a $1.4 billion saving

2014. This was driven by the fact 
the Oneworld carrier remains the 

only major US airline without fuel 
hedges – a practice that has 
proved lucrative in recent months.

“We continue to see a material 

financial benefit resulting from the 
steep year-over-year decline in

hedged,” says Derek Kerr, chief fi-
nancial officer of American.

United Airlines’ consolidated 

fuel costs fell 36% to $1.86 billion 
during the quarter. However, its

million after it settled a hedge

losing currency

basis, its operating result in the 
first quarter improved €109 mil-
lion, but accounting for currency, 
it cut losses only €29 million. “As 
demonstrated in the first quarter, 
almost all of the expected savings 
on the fuel bill could be offset by 
unit revenue pressure and nega-
tive currency impacts,” it says.

Currency played a similar role 

across European carriers during 
the quarter, tempering their pro-
gress. Collectively, European air-

challenging first quarter by $400 
million. Net losses were cut even 
more, although this was distorted 
by one-off gains at Lufthansa re-
lated to its JetBlue stake.

first profit for the period.

The re-entry of widebodies on United’s short-haul routes could signal twin-aisle comeback in the USA

Delta Air Lines

GOiNG laRGE scalE

U

nited Airlines’ decision 
to re-introduce wide-
bodies on regular US 
domestic services in the 

coming years is the latest step in 
the shift to larger-gauge aircraft 
across the US industry.

The carrier plans to use 10 Boe-

ing 777-200s, which will be refur-
bished for domestic service from 
their present international configu-
ration, to consolidate frequencies 
between its seven domestic hubs 
and to Hawaii. The move, revealed 
by United chief financial officer 
John Rainey, will free up mainline 
narrowbodies to offset the depar-
ture of more than 130 50-seat re-
gional jets by the end of this year, 
and more as aircraft come off leases 
in the years to come.

 “These fleet changes allow us 

to reduce our reliance on 50-seat 
regional jets from approximately 
8% of our ASMs at the beginning 
of last year to about 4% by the end 
of next year,” he says.

United, along with American 

Airlines and Delta Air Lines, 
plans to remove about 447 small 
regional jets in favour of 76-seat 
models, and even more small 
mainline aircraft by the end of 
2015, Flightglobal estimates.

American is focused on adding 

new Bombardier CRJ900s and Em-
braer 175s, as well as shifting to-
wards larger Airbus A321s and 
Boeing 737-800s from Airbus 

A320s and Boeing MD-80s in its 
mainline fleet.

At the same time, Delta is in the 

process of adding up to 70 

 

CRJ900s to its feeder fleet and 91 
Boeing 717-200s with 110 seats to 
its mainline fleet.

ANCILLARY BENEFITS

The shift to larger-gauge aircraft re-
duces unit costs, improves opera-
tional reliability with fewer flights 
and increases the potential to gen-
erate ancillary revenue, executives 
at all three airlines have said.

These benefits and United’s an-

nouncement beg the question – 
are we on the cusp of a widebody 
renaissance in the USA? The an-
swer so far is unclear.

Delta says it already operates 16

used on flights from its Atlanta 
and Detroit hubs to the US West 
Coast during the northern summer 
and from Detroit and Minneapo-
lis-Saint Paul to Florida during 
the northern winter.

American does not regularly op-

erate any widebody aircraft on do-
mestic routes, beyond positioning 
flights and the occasional opera-
tional recovery flight.

“We certainly aren’t going to

cally,” says American’s president 
Scott Kirby. “It’s lower cost to pro-
vide two narrowbodies than it is 
to provide a single widebody for 
the same number of seats.”

It “makes all the economic 

sense” to upgauge within an aircraft 
class – for example from 50 to 76 
seats or from an A320 to A321 – but 
not to widebodies, he says.

The door on domestic widebod-

ies at American, if not slammed 
shut, certainly appears closed for 
now. Domestic widebodies were 
once a mainstay of longer and high-

density domestic flights in the USA. 
They were regularly used by the 
major carriers on transcontinental 
and busy domestic routes like Chi-
cago-New York until the market 
shifted to narrowbodies and in-
creased frequency in the 1990s.

Even American operated Boeing 

767-200ERs between New York JFK 
and both Los Angeles and San 
Francisco until it replaced them 
with A321s in January 2014.

757-200s and the aforementioned 
777s on international routes.

FLIGHT-TRACKING

The disappearance of MH370 has led to the development of a number of systems promising better 
tracking of aircraft location, but the ability to disable the technology remains a troublesome issue

KEEPING ACTIVE

 M

alaysia Airlines is in-
troducing a flight-
tracking capability 
this summer, just 

over a year since the disappear-
ance of its MH370 service to Bei-
jing spurred a proliferation of 
technologies aimed at ensuring 
aircraft locations can be perma-
nently monitored.

But while flight-tracking is being 

marketed as an advancement in 
safety, the dilemma centred on the 
capability to disable such systems 
remains an uncomfortable – and so 
far unresolved – aspect.

Malaysia Airlines’ implementa-

tion of SITA OnAir’s Aircom 
FlightTracking service follows the 
loss of MH370 and its 239 occu-
pants in March last year. No trace 
of the Boeing 777-200ER has been 
found, despite an extensive ocean 
search off western Australia.

SURVEILLANCE INTERVAL

Navigation organisation Airser-
vices Australia is leading a test of 
enhanced oceanic flight-tracking 
– in which Malaysian authorities 
will participate – aimed at assess-
ing a new 15min minimum sur-
veillance interval agreed during a 
high-level ICAO gathering in Feb-
ruary. This interval will shorten to 
5min or less if an aircraft deviates 
unexpectedly from track by 200ft 
in altitude or 2nm.

Satellite communications spe-

cialist Inmarsat, which uncovered 
a few brief transmissions that gave 
the only clue to MH370’s location, 
is supporting the test. But Airser-
vices chair Angus Houston has 
cautioned that deliberate interfer-
ence – already strongly suspected 
by investigators examining 
MH370’s loss – could easily un-
dermine flight-tracking efforts if 
the capability exists to disable on-
board communications links, in-
cluding the ADS-C transmissions 
that the test will be based on.

“I think we’ve got to be very, 

very careful,” he said during a 
briefing on the test, “because you 
can turn this system off.”

“While the system was operat-

ing, we’d know exactly where the 
aircraft was. But if somebody had 
turned the system off… we’re in 
the same set of circumstances as 
we’ve experienced [with MH370].”

IATA’s aircraft tracking task 

force has been unable to offer rec-
onciliation. “All electrical compo-
nents on board an aircraft must 
have the ability to have their 
power source interrupted in the 
event of an electrical system mal-
function or fire,” it says. “While 
these types of operational and 
safety-related events are rare, the 

fact remains that equipment on 
board aircraft can be disabled.”

US National Transportation 

Safety Board investigators recom-
mended earlier this year that air-
craft operating extended over-wa-
ter routes should be fitted with 
“tamper-resistant” technology to 
identify position, down to a radius 
of 6nm, in the event of a loss.

Malaysia Airlines has already 

adopted measures to improve sit-
uation awareness, halving the po-
sition-reporting interval for its 777 
fleet to 15min and cutting it to 
10min for other types.

Investigators have yet to deter-

mine the reasons behind MH370’s 
disappearance. But the sudden 
absence of radio communications, 
secondary radar returns and 
ADS-B transmissions during an 
apparent controlled deviation 
from the aircraft’s assigned course 
has left the inquiry with few ra-
tional explanations outside of de-
liberate commandeering.

One firm aiming to solve the de-

activation problem is US-based 

InFlight Labs, which is seeking to 
develop a passive system that de-
tects transponder disengagement 
or failure and responds with an 
independent data transmission.

LOCATING ACCIDENTS

While deliberate deactivation 
would render tracking systems ir-
relevant, they would nevertheless 
potentially serve to speed up the 
process of locating an accident. 
Although a 15min position-re-
porting interval would leave more 
uncertainty than existed after the 
loss of Air France flight AF447 in 
2009, automatic triggering of 
shorter intervals, typically 1min, 
would reduce this uncertainty to a 
level compatible with the 6nm ra-
dius sought by investigators.

SITA OnAir is working on a ca-

pability to detect and report unu-
sual situations to complement its 
tracking initiative. “We are also 
investigating new aircraft solu-
tions that are independent of air-
craft power or systems,” says chief 
executive Ian Dawkins.

Multiple tracking options have 

emerged in the wake of MH370’s 
disappearance. Rockwell Collins 
has developed ARINC MultiLink, 
which merges data from several 
surveillance sources – among 
them radar, high-frequency data-
link, ADS-B and ADS-C, and 
ACARS – to give carriers a “com-
prehensive” and “robust” picture 
of their fleet, it says. 

United Technologies is offering 

a position-reporting feature tied to 
its Aircraft Data Management line, 
which includes its electronic flight-
bag systems, while Canadian man-
ufacturer FLYHT’s automated re-
porting system AFIRS has location 
capability that also includes flight-
data streaming in the event of an 
abnormal occurrence.

Another Canadian-led initiative, 

the Aireon partnership, is pursuing 
a space-based ADS-B capability 
due to become available in 2017.
