ECONOMICS BRIEFING 

IMPACT OF REDUCING CROWN RENTS ON 
TORONTO PEARSON INTERNATIONAL AIRPORT  

KEY CONCLUSIONS 

ä 

The proposed reduction in crown rents at Toronto Pearson International Airport (YYZ) of $3.72 per arriving 

passenger would reduce landing fees by 13% and a typical C$200 ticket for short-haul travel by almost 2%;  

ä 

Arriving passengers at YYZ would be boosted by 2.2% or more than 340,000 a year; 

ä 

The fiscal impact of the proposed reduction in crown rents of an average C$57.6 million a year would be 

offset by the extra C$62 million in tax revenues generated from the economic activity associated with the 
additional airport passengers; 

ä 

There would also be wider economic benefits including an estimated additional C$300 million in business 

revenues, 3000 jobs and C$80 million wage income in the southern Ontario economy. 

ANALYSIS 

ä 

This note uses available analyses to estimate the economic and tax revenue impact of reducing crown rents 

on YYZ, as proposed by Transport Pearson

1

. 

ä 

The Transport Pearson (

ibid

) proposal reduces rent payments during 2006-2010 by C$288 million as 

compared to the existing ground lease.  Assuming a straight-line reduction of C$ 57.6 million a year that 

implies a reduction for each of the 15.5 million passengers arriving at YYZ

2

 of C$3.72

. 

ä 

This would produce a 

13% reduction in landing fees

 at YYZ per arriving passenger and a 

reduction of 

almost 2% on a typical C$200 ticket for short-haul travel;

 

ä 

The 

increasing sensitivity of airline passengers to the price of a ticket

 is widely documented

3

, including 

a recent literature review by the Department of Finance in Ottawa.  Liberalisation of air transport markets in 
N America has led to a dramatic increase in competition between airlines and choice for the consumer.  New 
low cost entrants and innovation by incumbent carriers has led to a substantial increase in the ‘no frills/low 
fare’ segment of the market, which is highly price sensitive.  Moreover, the air fare transparency delivered by 
the internet and new corporate travel buyers has meant that the previously less price sensitive business 
segment of the air travel market is now much more responsive to price changes.  Past estimates (

ibid

) that 

short-haul leisure passengers have a price elasticity of 1.5 (will fly 15% more if prices fall 10%) is probably a 
lower bound of today’s situation in Canadian air travel markets; 

ä 

As a result we use the results of a recent econometric study

4

 which measured the 

impact on air traffic 

traffic in Canada of the aviation security charge (ATSC)

 of C$12, using detailed origin-destination 

passenger and yield data.  On short-haul travel (43% of YYZ’s 2005 passengers were domestic) traffic fell 
11% as prices paid by passengers rose 5.9% as a result of the security charge.  On medium/long haul routes 
traffic fell 4.4%.

Impact of reducing crown rents on Toronto Pearson  International Airport

 

ä 

The proposed reduction in crown rents is estimated to be around C$3.72 per arriving passenger, equivalent 

to 30% of the ATSC (there is no reason to think passengers will not react symetrically to a fall as to a rise in 
the price of travel).  On that basis YYZ might expect to see short-haul traffic rise 3.3% and medium/long-haul 
by 1.3%.  That implies 

the crown rent reduction would boost overall passenger arrivals by 2.2% or 

over 340,000 each year

; 

ä 

YYZ’s economic impact in southern Ontario

 is estimated

5

 to already generate: 

o 

C$14 billion in business revenue 

o 

C$3.6 billion in wages 

o 

C$2.8 billion in tax revenue 

o 

138,000 jobs 

ä 

Since this impact is generated using a broadly linear input-output model of the economy it should be a valid 

approximation to estimate the incremental economic impact of reducing crown rents by the incremental 
increase in passenger arrivals.  It follows 

that if crown rents were reduced as proposed, producing a 

2.2% increase in the number of passenger arrivals, there would be the following benefits for the 
southern Ontario economy

: 

o 

Tax revenues would rise C$62 million a year 

o 

Business revenues would be boosted by C$300 million a year 

o 

An extra 3,000 jobs would be created 

o 

Wage income would rise by C$80 million a year
