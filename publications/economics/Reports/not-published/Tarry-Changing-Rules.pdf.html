ANALYST VIEWPOINT

 

JULY 2011

 

 

NEW RULES OF ENGAGEMENT – OR MORE OF THE SAME? 

 

CHRIS TARRY, AVIATION INDUSTRY RESEARCH AND ANALYSIS (CTAIRA) 

The outcome for any business is the result of large numbers often moving in different directions and in 
this respect the airline industry is no different. Against the unsurprising background where IATA’s latest 
forecast contained a further downward move, attention has inevitably focused yet again not only on what 
is perhaps best described as “event related disturbance” – whether that be; ash clouds, a step change in 
economic activity or in this particular case the fuel price.  But it has also turned focus on to issues of 
resilience and again the extent to which individual airlines can protect, rather than insulate themselves 
against the vagaries of the real world. The rather straightforward but not particularly helpful answer to this 
is that “it depends”.  

Tony Tyler, the incoming Director General of IATA during his acceptance speech at the recent AGM 
seemed to put things into sharp relief when he said that “the need is to keep revenues ahead of costs” 
which is in fact rather more difficult than it might seem. Whilst there is a healthy focus on having the best 
cost base possible the reality is that swings in the fuel price on one side of the equation and changes both 
underlying revenue (due to the economic environment) and an inability to increase revenue to recover 
higher costs (more a function of an imbalance between supply and demand) on the other lie outside 
management control.

Put in the simplest terms 
any company’s strategy 
has three elements; to 
survive, compete and 
prosper and given that 
there are only a 
decreasing number of 
monopoly routes, the best 
outcome is probably one 
that is best described as 
“competitive prosperity”. 

Given the recent financial 
performance of the airline 
industry it is perhaps of 
interest to consider 
whether what we are 
seeing in 2011 is in fact a 
repeat of history where a 
high fuel price and where 
in some markets too much 
capacity will prove particularly testing or whether it is just what might be described as a bump on the road 
to this desired state of “competitive prosperity”. Since the start of the year I have seen statements from 
companies that range from expressing greater caution about the outlook to ones that are outright profit 
warnings. I have also seen the analyst community continue to downgrade most of their forecasts, reduce 
target prices and push the time period when the target price will be reached further into the future. 

At the heart of any discussion over forecasting there is a debate concerning whether history is in fact a 
good guide to the future. Indeed forecasting in some areas becomes a little difficult if it isn’t already for, 
although one might be able to derive a series of relationships between how particular variables change, 
the extent of the movement becomes more difficult to predict. In this respect it is interesting to look at the 
performance of the airline industry; over the period from the late 1970s until 2003 the airline business 
cycle had a reasonably distinctive pattern of 7-8 years of profit and 3 of loss with the peak in the 5

th

 or 6

th

 

year of the upswing before a relatively rapid decline into loss. However since 2004 quite different pattern 
has been evident where there were 4 years of profit and two years of losses before 2010’s record result. 
With hindsight the outcome for 2010 was the result of the coincidence of a particularly favourable set of 
circumstances driven by a sharp recovery firstly in cargo and then in premium traffic against a background 
where capacity has been taken out of the market in the downturn and fuel for the most part was still 
“manageable”. Whilst there is still “downside risk” to the actual outcome for 2011 I still expect a profit to be 
reported at the industry level – however managements are having to react to a new set of circumstances. 
As I often commented, the path from the last downturn to the next peak was never going to be a straight 
line even against the background of a shorter cycle (which is not necessarily going to be the case) - and 
that was before the “distortion” of 2010’s outcome – in reality 2011 is the second year of the recovery from 
the 2008-09 downturn. 

In terms of forecasting what you are inevitably doing is taking a snapshot of a set of possible outcomes 
that are linked to a series of assumptions and the closeness of the forecast outcomes to reality depends 
entirely on whether the assumptions themselves are not just realistic but reflect the summation on the one 
hand of a whole series of economic factors, the net effect of a whole series of business decisions on the 
other and is also able to take into account a series of what appear to be essentially random movements in

Clearly other costs have been rising too as the chart suggests and although the change in the fuel price 
has had a significant impact in some cases it is not the only issue. 

In terms of relating the increase in fuel to the increase in revenue (chart 4) to provide an indication of 
recoverability then it is a pretty mixed picture - here the time horizons for the results are also important. 

Results however only tell us what has happened and if there are some interesting statistics that emerge 
also give us something to follow and track into the future. Furthermore it is always the trend that is more 
interesting and important and here as I have suggested in the past only when we see some more results 
will we be able to determine whether the “trend is our friend” or, on the basis of the evidence here if it 
continues in this way, then perhaps not.  

At the risk of being boring, an increase in fares whether to recover higher fuel costs or not has a better 
chance of sticking where supply is constrained relative to demand – in this respect in the European short 
haul market for example fares are under pressure and reflect too many seats in a relatively weak demand 
environment - here a number of LCCs have resorted to increasing non-discretionary “ancillaries” to 
recover higher fuel costs. More encouraging is the news from Ryanair that they are planning to ground up 
to 80 aircraft this winter – of course we will only see after the event how much capacity was taken out. In 
part this reflects a capacity adjustment which should be positive for fares but equally it reflects the likely 
winter environment in this region where given the fuel price and the increase in other variable costs 
airlines will lose more by flying some services rather than by not flying them. Striking the right balance 
between demand and supply remains a necessary condition for a “better outcome”.  Against this 
background I am sure that we are not alone in tracking and analysing the winter season capacity 
announcements – in this respect we have been here before and certainly will be again in the future – the 
real issue is how long the capacity stays out of the market. This also takes us into the wider area of 
aircraft supply and demand and how that might adjust and where the risks are, but that is an issue for 
another day.
