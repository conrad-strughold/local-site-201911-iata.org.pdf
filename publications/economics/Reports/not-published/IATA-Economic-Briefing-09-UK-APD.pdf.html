ANALYSIS OF THE IMPACT OF A NEW RISE IN 
UK AIR PASSENGER DUTY 

 

KEY CONCLUSIONS 

Ê

 

On average, the increase in Air Passenger Duty (APD) would raise the cost of travel from the UK by 2.4% in 

2010-11. But long-haul travel will be more severely affected.  This would mean, for example, that passengers 
travelling in economy class for leisure or business from the UK to China would have to pay an extra 4.3% on top 
of their fare.  Travel volumes on markets like this would be reduced 3.6%, unless airlines absorbed the increased 
travel costs in lower profit margins. 

Ê

 

We estimate that the increased APD will reduce the number of passengers by 1.1 million by 2010-11 period, 

representing 1.5% of total passenger numbers.  

Ê

 

The effect of raising APD will adversely impact not only the aviation industry – with £700 million of revenues lost 

- but also will cause economic damage to the wider UK economy. Oxford Economics’ study (2006) implies that a 
reduction of passenger travel of this size would put some 8,000 UK jobs at risk.

ANALYSIS  

Ê

 

HM Treasury increased APD rates effective 1

st

 November 2009. This change affects all passengers carriage 

irrespective of when the ticket for travel was booked or purchased. Destinations are categorised into four 
geographical bands based on distance between London and the capital city of the destination country. 

Ê

 

There are two rates for each band, one for standard class and the other band for lowest class fares, as shown in 

the table below.  In 2010-11, the increased APD rates will range from £27 to £93, depending on the class of 
travel and destination. With an average return fare, including fuel surcharge, of £450, the increase of the APD 
rates would cost the average passenger an extra £12, which represent about 2.4% of the travel cost.  

Ê

 

Research (see Ref.1) on air travel demand price elasticities provides estimates on the extent to which 

passenger numbers may be reduced by raising the cost of air travel. This study shows price elasticities varying 
between -0.64 and -1.17, depending on the geographical market serving the UK. Using these price elasticities, 
we estimated the following impacts:  

o

  Passenger numbers could fall by 1.1 million in 2010-11, relative to the number that would have travelled 

before raising APD rates. This corresponds to a reduction of the number of passengers by about 1.5% 
in 2010-11.

Ê

 

If we assume the average cost to the UK economy of reduced passenger numbers is equal to the marginal cost 

then using the estimates provided by the Oxford Economics study (2006) it follows that the estimated 1.5% loss 
of passengers, resulting from the increased APD rates, will cause a direct loss of £129 million to aviation industry 
value-added and UK GDP in 2010-11 and some 8,000 jobs will be at risk within the aviation industry supply 
chain. 

Ê

 

In reality the marginal cost of lost passengers may be less than the average. However, the estimates of 

economic damage above do not include other damaging wider economic impacts to business travel, 
international trade and investment.
