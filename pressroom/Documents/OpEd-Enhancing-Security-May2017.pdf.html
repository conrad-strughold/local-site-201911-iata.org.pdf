Industry Focus

Enhancing Security

Through Global Partnership

By Matthew Vaughan

F

lying is safe. And flying is secure. We 
know that. On average, each day, an air 
transport workforce of 8 million people 

supports 100,000 commercial flights that col-
lectively carry over 10 million passengers safely 
to their destination. Yet, it is a sad reality of 
modern life that terrorists continue to target 
public spaces and transportation systems. 

Last year, two airports suffered terrorist attacks 
with many innocent lives lost. Also last year, 
passengers on Daallo Airlines flight 159 nar-
rowly escaped disaster when a bomb carried 
on board exploded. Fortunately, the aircraft fly-
ing at a lower altitude and the pilot was able to 
land the airplane safely. 

Staying one step ahead of those with an agen-
da of evil is a challenge, the scale of which is 
massive. Recognizing this, last September, the 
UN Security Council passed Resolution 2309 
challenging ICAO to deliver a Global Aviation 
Security Plan (GASeP). The resolution made 
clear that security is a government responsi-
bility. However, for security to be effective co-
operation and partnership among all aviation 
stakeholders is essential. 

IATA has identified five goals:

 

The implementation of global aviation secu-
rity standards 

 

Enhanced information sharing among avia-
tion stakeholders 

 

The development of standards to tackle 
emerging threats  

 

The adoption of a risk-based approach to 
tacking security threats 

 

The promotion of a security culture

Implementation of Global standards  

The nearly 100,000 flights that will operate to-
day can do so only because the rules are basi-
cally the same across their networks. Likewise, 
global standards must be implemented in se-
curity systems globally and strictly enforced 
through a robust audit processes. 

Today this is not always the case. States with 
identified significant security concerns are not 
always held accountable. The ICAO Universal 
Security Audit Program (USAP) used to mea-
sure States’ level of compliance to Annex 17 
security standards needs to be urgently im-
proved. The industry has a chance to achieve 
this through the development of clear strategic 
goals, backed up by ambitious and measurable 
targets in the GASeP.

security threat Information sharing 

In July 2017, it will be 3 years since the shoot-
ing down of MH17 and governments are still 
not sharing the required level of actionable se-
curity intelligence needed to effectively support 
air carrier risk assessments. ICAO’s resulting 
Conflict Zone Repository not deemed effective. 

The recent implementation of restrictions on the 
carry-on of large electronic devices on flights 
originating at certain airports in the Middle East 
on direct flights to the United States and the 
UK is a further example of a lack of information 
sharing. There was no prior consultation with 
airlines and little coordination by governments. 
Furthermore, the ban raised questions as to the 
safety risk associated with the increased stor-
age of lithium battery powered devices in the 
cargo hold of aircraft. Intelligence information 
sharing saves lives. There can be no anti-com-
petitive and liable concerns when information 
is shared on this basis. If airlines understand 
the outcome governments are trying to achieve 
they can help with their operational experience 
to deliver that result effectively and efficiently. 

Emerging threats 

Terrorists continue to search for new ways to 
do harm. The threat of insider acts and Cyber-
related threats underscore the imperative for 
airlines to report occurrences, share best prac-
tices and continually evolve the notion of se-
curity culture within an integrated safety and 
security management approach.  

risk Based Approach

Adopting a risk-based approach to security – 
making best use of technology and intelligence 
to ensure that limited resources are applied 
where the risk is greatest - is the key to sustain-
able security improvements. 

Today over 45 states require airlines to collect 
advance passenger information (API) or trans-
mit passenger name record (PNR) data—or 
both. A slightly larger number of states are plan-

ning to introduce such requirements. This is a 
great indication that governments are putting in 
place the systems to support a risk-based ap-
proach and strengthen their borders. 

But we should not stop there. The use of reg-
istered traveler programs for immigration and 
security needs to be broadened. The success 
of current known traveler programs tells us that 
passengers are willing to share more data in or-
der to smooth airport processes. Today we are 
starting to see some of these programs being 
connected across borders. When this happens 
more broadly it will fuel a virtuous cycle of in-
creasing incentives for more people to join the 
programs, resulting in more knowledge about 
who is traveling and even better security.

security culture

Promotion of a security culture is essential. 
Creating a strong security culture enables the 
entire aviation community to be proactive in the 
delivery of its approach to security.

It is vital that as an industry we invest properly 
in developing the next generation of security 
leaders. Governments face the same issue. In 
the same way that airlines and airports must 
build specialized security skills, regulators will 
also need to develop specialists to confront cy-
ber and physical threats in the aviation context.

Moving forward

The common vision among industry and regu-
lators is a data-driven risk-based and outcome-
focused security delivered in partnership with 
industry and to harmonized global standards. 
The challenge is to work together to deliver 
it while staying one step ahead of those who 
would choose to do our industry harm. Security 
is not a destination it’s a journey.
